# Covariance stationary

**Program:** CFA® Program
**Level:** Level II

## Definition

Describes a time series when its expected value and variance are constant and finite in all periods and when its covariance with itself for a fixed number of periods in the past or future is constant and finite in all periods.

---

## The Real-World Analogy

Imagine you're trying to predict the daily routine of a friend. If your friend has a *stable personality* — they wake up around 7 a.m. give or take an hour, get equally moody on Mondays as on Fridays, and the way today's mood relates to yesterday's mood is the same pattern you saw last year — then you can confidently forecast their behavior next Tuesday.

Now imagine instead your friend is going through a turbulent life change: their wake-up time is creeping later every month, their moods swing wildly some weeks and barely at all in others, and the link between consecutive days keeps shifting. You cannot forecast this person — not because they're unknowable, but because **the rules describing them keep changing**.

That's what covariance stationarity is: a guarantee that the statistical "rules" generating a time series are *not drifting over time*. Without that guarantee, a model fit to the past will mis-predict the future, because the past was generated by a different process than the present.

---

## The Three Formal Conditions

A time series $\{x_t\}$ is **covariance stationary** (also called *weakly stationary* or *second-order stationary*) if all three of the following hold for every time period $t$:

| # | Condition | Formal Statement | Plain Reading |
|---|-----------|------------------|---------------|
| 1 | **Constant, finite mean** | $E[x_t] = \mu$ for all $t$ | The series hovers around one fixed long-run average — no upward or downward drift. |
| 2 | **Constant, finite variance** | $\text{Var}(x_t) = \sigma^2$ for all $t$ | The "wobble" around the mean is the same size in every period — no calm stretches followed by violent ones. |
| 3 | **Covariance depends only on lag** | $\text{Cov}(x_t, x_{t-k}) = \gamma_k$ for all $t$, depends only on $k$ | The relationship between two observations is determined by *how far apart* they are, not *when* they happened. |

The third condition is the subtle one. It says: the way today's value relates to yesterday's value (lag 1) must be the *same* relationship that 1995's value had to 1994's value. Same for lag 2, lag 3, and so on. The connections are pinned to the *gap*, not the *calendar date*.

---

## Why Each Condition Matters for AR Models

The whole point of covariance stationarity is that it's the **precondition for using autoregressive (AR) models**. An AR(1) model takes the form:

$$x_t = b_0 + b_1 x_{t-1} + \varepsilon_t$$

This says today's value is a constant plus a slope times yesterday's value plus random noise. The model is estimated using **Ordinary Least Squares (OLS)** regression, and OLS only produces valid coefficients under specific assumptions. Each stationarity condition maps directly onto one of those assumptions.

### Why constant mean matters

An AR(1) with $|b_1| < 1$ has a well-defined long-run mean:

$$\mu = \frac{b_0}{1 - b_1}$$

This is the value the series gravitates toward — its "home base." If the *true* mean of the data drifts over time, then $\mu$ isn't a fixed number anymore, and the model has nothing to anchor its forecasts to. You'd be fitting one equation to a target that's actually moving. **Why?** Because $b_0$ and $b_1$ are estimated as constants; if the mean drifts, those constants can't simultaneously fit the early portion and the late portion of the data.

### Why constant variance matters

OLS regression produces standard errors — the basis for every t-statistic, p-value, and confidence interval — under the assumption that the residual variance is constant (homoscedasticity). If the variance of $x_t$ itself drifts (say, it gets bigger over time), then the residuals will too, and every inference statistic the model produces is biased. **Why?** Because the formula for the standard error of $\hat{b}_1$ assumes one $\sigma^2$ for the whole sample — feeding it a series whose volatility doubled halfway through gives a number that doesn't describe either half correctly. See [[Homoscedasticity and Heteroscedasticity]].

### Why constant covariance matters

The slope $b_1$ in an AR(1) is essentially the lag-1 autocorrelation of the series — it measures how strongly past values pull on present values. If that strength itself changes over time (e.g., the series was strongly mean-reverting in the 1990s but became a random walk in the 2010s), then a single $b_1$ can't capture the truth. **Why?** Because $b_1$ is a *summary* of the relationship between $x_t$ and $x_{t-1}$ across the entire sample; if the relationship varies by period, the summary is meaningless for forecasting.

---

## What Goes Wrong If a Series Is Not Stationary

Forcing an AR model onto a non-stationary series produces three classes of failure:

1. **Invalid statistical inference.** T-statistics and p-values are computed assuming constant variance and stable parameters. When those assumptions break, the t-stat for $b_1$ might say the coefficient is "highly significant" when in truth it's no different from zero. You'd reject a null hypothesis you should have accepted.

2. **Spurious regressions.** Two unrelated trending series — say, the price of tea in China and the number of U.S. divorces — can produce an enormous, "highly significant" $R^2$ when regressed on each other. Both are simply rising over time; there's no actual economic link. See [[CFA_Glossary/Spurious correlation]]. This is the most dangerous failure because the model *looks* impressive.

3. **Undefined long-run behavior.** A non-stationary series like a random walk has variance that grows toward infinity ($\text{Var}(x_t) = t \cdot \sigma_\varepsilon^2$). There's no fixed mean to revert to, no stable variance to bound the forecast interval. Any forecast horizon longer than a few steps becomes useless because the confidence band widens without limit.

## How to Detect Non-Stationarity

### Visual inspection (always step one)

Plot the series against time. Ask three questions:

- **Trend?** Does the series drift visibly upward or downward? → Mean is not constant.
- **Funnel shape?** Does the spread of values widen (or narrow) as time goes on? → Variance is not constant.
- **Regime shifts?** Does the behavior look qualitatively different in one stretch versus another (e.g., flat then volatile)? → Covariance structure is shifting.

```
Stationary series:                      Non-stationary (trending):

  |  ~~  ~~~ ~  ~~~ ~                      |                    ~~~
  | ~  ~~   ~ ~~   ~~                      |                ~~~~
  |~~  ~~~~~ ~~  ~~~                       |            ~~~~
  |  ~~  ~~~ ~  ~~~ ~                      |        ~~~~
  |__________________ time                 |____~~~~_______________ time
   ↑ hovers around fixed mean               ↑ mean is drifting
```

### Formal: the Dickey-Fuller test

Visual inspection can mislead. The formal tool is the **Dickey-Fuller test**, which checks for a *unit root* — a specific mathematical condition that makes a series non-stationary.

The test starts from the AR(1):

$$x_t = b_0 + b_1 x_{t-1} + \varepsilon_t$$

Subtract $x_{t-1}$ from both sides:

$$x_t - x_{t-1} = b_0 + (b_1 - 1)x_{t-1} + \varepsilon_t$$

Let $g_1 = b_1 - 1$. The test becomes:

$$\Delta x_t = b_0 + g_1 x_{t-1} + \varepsilon_t$$

The hypotheses are:

- $H_0: g_1 = 0$ (equivalently $b_1 = 1$ — there is a unit root, the series is non-stationary)
- $H_a: g_1 < 0$ (equivalently $b_1 < 1$ — the series is stationary)

The trick: the t-statistic on $\hat{g}_1$ does not follow a standard t-distribution under $H_0$, so Dickey and Fuller derived their own critical values. If the computed t-stat is more negative than the critical value, you reject $H_0$ and conclude the series is stationary.

## Common Remedies

If a series fails stationarity, you transform it until it passes, then model the transformed series.

### First-differencing — the workhorse fix

Instead of modeling the level $x_t$, model the *change*:

$$y_t = x_t - x_{t-1} = \Delta x_t$$

This usually kills a linear trend. Why? If $x_t$ is rising by roughly the same amount each period, then $\Delta x_t$ is roughly constant — and *constant* is stationary. After differencing, you fit an AR model to $\{y_t\}$, then if you need a forecast in original units, you cumulate the differences back.

A series that becomes stationary after one round of differencing is called **integrated of order 1**, or $I(1)$.

### Log transformation — for exponential growth

If a series is growing at a roughly constant *percentage* rate (compound growth — GDP, stock indices), the level grows exponentially but the *log* of the level grows linearly. Take $\ln(x_t)$ first, then first-difference if needed:

$$y_t = \ln(x_t) - \ln(x_{t-1}) \approx \text{percentage change in } x_t$$

This is why returns (log differences of prices) are typically stationary even when prices themselves are not.

### Decision flow

```
Plot the series
      │
      ▼
Trend visible? ──Yes──► First-difference ──► Plot again ──► Test (DF)
      │
      No
      ▼
Variance growing? ──Yes──► Take logs first, then difference if needed
      │
      No
      ▼
Run Dickey-Fuller anyway as a sanity check
```

## Covariance Stationary vs. Strictly Stationary

The CFA curriculum uses *covariance stationary*, but you should know the stricter cousin exists.

| Property | **Covariance (Weakly) Stationary** | **Strictly Stationary** |
|----------|-----------------------------------|------------------------|
| What's required to be constant | Mean, variance, and lag-$k$ covariances only | The *entire joint distribution* of any collection of observations |
| Practical use | Standard requirement for AR/MA/ARMA models | Mostly a theoretical construct |
| If data is normal | Equivalent to strict stationarity | (Same — normal distributions are pinned down by mean and variance) |
| Used in CFA | **Yes** — this is the working definition | Mentioned for completeness only |

For non-normal data, strict stationarity is stronger. But because we typically work under approximate normality, the CFA curriculum treats covariance stationarity as the operative condition.

## Worked Numerical Example

Let's compare two AR(1) processes side by side, starting from $x_0 = 0$, with $b_0 = 2$, and the same noise sequence $\varepsilon_t$ in each. The only difference is the slope $b_1$.

**Series A (stationary):** $b_1 = 0.5$
**Series B (non-stationary):** $b_1 = 1.0$ (a random walk with drift)

Suppose $\varepsilon_t$ takes the values $\{1, -2, 3, -1, 2, -3, 1, 2, -1, 3\}$ for $t = 1, \dots, 10$.

### Series A: $x_t = 2 + 0.5 x_{t-1} + \varepsilon_t$

| $t$ | $x_{t-1}$ | $0.5 x_{t-1}$ | $\varepsilon_t$ | $x_t = 2 + 0.5x_{t-1} + \varepsilon_t$ |
|-----|-----------|---------------|-----------------|----------------------------------------|
| 1 | 0.000 | 0.000 | 1 | **3.000** |
| 2 | 3.000 | 1.500 | -2 | **1.500** |
| 3 | 1.500 | 0.750 | 3 | **5.750** |
| 4 | 5.750 | 2.875 | -1 | **3.875** |
| 5 | 3.875 | 1.938 | 2 | **5.938** |
| 6 | 5.938 | 2.969 | -3 | **1.969** |
| 7 | 1.969 | 0.984 | 1 | **3.984** |
| 8 | 3.984 | 1.992 | 2 | **5.992** |
| 9 | 5.992 | 2.996 | -1 | **3.996** |
| 10 | 3.996 | 1.998 | 3 | **6.998** |

The values bounce around in a band roughly between 1.5 and 7. The theoretical long-run mean is

$$\mu = \frac{b_0}{1 - b_1} = \frac{2}{1 - 0.5} = 4$$

— and indeed the values cluster around 4. Each shock decays: a $+3$ shock at $t=3$ contributes $3$ at $t=3$, $1.5$ at $t=4$, $0.75$ at $t=5$, … shrinking geometrically. The series has memory, but the memory fades.

### Series B: $x_t = 2 + 1.0 \cdot x_{t-1} + \varepsilon_t$

| $t$ | $x_{t-1}$ | $1.0 \cdot x_{t-1}$ | $\varepsilon_t$ | $x_t = 2 + x_{t-1} + \varepsilon_t$ |
|-----|-----------|---------------------|-----------------|-------------------------------------|
| 1 | 0 | 0 | 1 | **3** |
| 2 | 3 | 3 | -2 | **3** |
| 3 | 3 | 3 | 3 | **8** |
| 4 | 8 | 8 | -1 | **9** |
| 5 | 9 | 9 | 2 | **13** |
| 6 | 13 | 13 | -3 | **12** |
| 7 | 12 | 12 | 1 | **15** |
| 8 | 15 | 15 | 2 | **19** |
| 9 | 19 | 19 | -1 | **20** |
| 10 | 20 | 20 | 3 | **25** |

The series marches steadily upward — by $t=10$ it's at 25 and climbing. Plug into the long-run mean formula:

$$\mu = \frac{2}{1 - 1} = \frac{2}{0} \quad \text{undefined}$$

There is no home base. Every shock is *permanent*: the $+3$ at $t=3$ raises the level by 3 forever, not just for one period. Variance compounds with each step:

$$\text{Var}(x_t) = t \cdot \sigma_\varepsilon^2$$

By $t=100$ the variance is 100× what it was at $t=1$. By $t=1000$, it's 1000×. There is no finite variance, no constant mean, and no stable covariance — every condition fails.

### What this means for forecasting

If you tried to fit an AR(1) to Series B and used it to forecast, the model would estimate $\hat{b}_1 \approx 1$, the t-stat would be enormous, the $R^2$ would be near 1, and **every forecast would be useless**. That's the tragedy of non-stationarity — the model output looks magnificent and is silently wrong.

The fix: first-difference Series B.

$$y_t = x_t - x_{t-1} = 2 + \varepsilon_t$$

The differenced series has mean 2, constant variance $\sigma_\varepsilon^2$, and zero autocorrelation — it's stationary white noise around a drift. Now an AR model on $\{y_t\}$ would correctly tell you "the change from period to period is centered on 2 with random shocks," which is the *truth* of how Series B was generated.
