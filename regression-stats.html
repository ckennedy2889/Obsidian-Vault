The Anatomy of a Regression Line

Six numbers do the heavy lifting when you fit a line to data. Here is what each one actually looks like — drawn, decomposed, and demystified.

Foundation

Three kinds of distance

Every point in your dataset has three vertical distances that matter. Understanding them is the whole game — SST, SSE, and SSR are just the squared versions, summed up.

Total (from ȳ) Explained (by line) Residual (left over)

Pick any observation y. Its distance from the mean ȳ is the total deviation. The regression line captures part of it — the explained part, ŷ − ȳ. Whatever is left between the point and the line is the residual, y − ŷ.

(y − ȳ) = (ŷ − ȳ) + (y − ŷ)

Square each distance, sum across all points, and you get the three sums of squares.

01

SST — the total variation in y

Σ(y−ȳ)²

SST (Sum of Squares Total) measures how much y jumps around its own mean. It has nothing to do with x or any model — it is simply the raw spread of the response variable. Think of it as the budget of variation you have to explain.

Formula SST = Σ (yᵢ − ȳ)²

Imagine a world where you have no predictors — your best guess for every y is just the mean ȳ. SST is how wrong that guess is, squared and summed. It is the ceiling: no model can explain more than SST.

SST is fixed by your data. Fit any model you like — SST does not change.
02

SSR — what the model explains

Σ(ŷ−ȳ)²

SSR (Sum of Squares Regression) — sometimes called SSM or the "explained" sum of squares — measures how much of y's variation the regression line accounts for. It is the distance from each prediction ŷ to the mean ȳ, squared and summed.

Formula SSR = Σ (ŷᵢ − ȳ)²

A flat regression line would sit right on ȳ and SSR would be zero — the model explains nothing. A steep line that tracks the data closely produces large (ŷ − ȳ) distances and a big SSR.

SSR = variation the model captures. Bigger SSR (relative to SST) means a better fit.
03

SSE — what the model misses

Σ(y−ŷ)²

SSE (Sum of Squares Error) — also called the residual sum of squares (RSS) — is the leftover. The gap between each actual point and where the line predicted it would be. Ordinary least squares is literally the procedure that minimizes SSE.

Formula SSE = Σ (yᵢ − ŷᵢ)²

Small SSE means the line hugs the data — the model is accurate. Large SSE means points are scattered far from predictions, and there is systematic structure the model failed to capture (or pure noise).

The fundamental identity: SST = SSR + SSE. Budget = Explained + Leftover.

04

R² — the share explained

SSR / SST

R² (coefficient of determination) is simply the fraction of SST that the model explains. Equivalently, one minus the fraction it leaves unexplained. It lives between 0 and 1 — the higher, the better the fit.

Formula R² = SSR / SST = 1 − (SSE / SST)

05

MSR & MSE — the mean squares

SS ÷ df

Sums of squares grow with sample size. To compare them fairly — and to build the F-test — we divide each by its degrees of freedom. The result is a mean square, which is basically an average squared deviation.

Mean Square Regression MSR = SSR / k
k = number of predictors

MSR is the average amount of variation explained per predictor. If you add a useful predictor, MSR climbs.

Mean Square Error MSE = SSE / (n − k − 1)
n = sample size

MSE is the average squared residual — the estimated variance of the errors σ². Its square root (RMSE) is in the original units of y and tells you typical prediction error.

F = MSR / MSE

The F-statistic for overall model significance is exactly this ratio — signal per predictor divided by noise per residual degree of freedom.

06

Adjusted R² — R² with a conscience

honest fit

Here is the dirty secret of R²: it never goes down when you add a predictor, even a useless one. Throw in random noise as a variable and R² either stays flat or creeps up. That makes plain R² a terrible tool for comparing models of different sizes.

Formula Adjusted R² = 1 − (1 − R²) × (n − 1) / (n − k − 1)

Adjusted R² penalizes you for each extra predictor. It only rises when a new variable helps more than you'd expect by chance. If you add junk, adjusted R² actually falls.
