## Why Monte Carlo?

> [!tip] Analogy
> The [[Binomial tree|binomial tree]] is like a subway map — a few fixed routes, neatly drawn. Monte Carlo is like releasing **thousands of GPS-tracked taxis** into the city and seeing where they all end up.

The [[2.03 - Binomial Interest Rate Tree|binomial tree]] has a fatal flaw for certain securities: it **recombines**. The up-then-down node merges with the down-then-up node — erasing the path history.

**Problem:** Some securities are **path-[[Dependent|dependent]]** — their cash flows depend on the *entire history* of rates, not just where rates are now.

---

## The Classic Example: Mortgage-Backed Securities

Homeowners can prepay their [[Mortgages|mortgages]] at any time. Their decision depends on:

1. **Current rates vs. [[Mortgage rate|mortgage rate]]** — if rates drop, refinancing makes sense
2. **The path rates took** — if rates dropped to 3% a year ago and are back at 5%, many homeowners *already* refinanced. The "easy refinancers" are gone.

> [!important] Prepayment Burnout
> This phenomenon — where prior low-rate periods exhaust the pool of willing refinancers — is called **prepayment burnout**. It makes MBS cash flows path-[[Dependent|dependent]].

**Why the tree can't handle this:** In a recombining tree, the "rates dropped then recovered" node is identical to the "rates rose then fell" node. But the MBS cash flows are *different* because the prepayment histories differ. Monte Carlo tracks each path individually.

## The 4 Steps of Monte Carlo Simulation

### Step 1: Generate Random Interest Rate Paths

A [[Random number|random number]]er generator|[[Random number|random number]] generator]] produces thousands of forward-rate paths (typically 1,000 to 10,000+). Each path is a complete sequence of 1-period rates from $t=0$ through maturity.

Rates evolve based on a [[2.08 - Term Structure Models|term structure model]]:

$$r_{t+1} = r_t + \text{drift}_t + \sigma \times \varepsilon_t$$

where $\varepsilon_t$ is a random draw from a [[Standard normal distribution|standard normal distribution]]on|normal distribution]].

### Step 2: Determine Cash Flows Along Each Path

For each path, calculate the bond's cash flows. For an MBS, this models **prepayment behavior** based on the entire rate history along that path.

### Step 3: Discount Each Path's Cash Flows

Same as [[2.06 - Pathwise Valuation|pathwise valuation]]:

$$V_{\text{path}\,i} = \sum_{t=1}^{T} \frac{CF_t}{\prod_{k=0}^{t-1}(1 + r_{k,i})}$$

### Step 4: Average All Path Values

$$\boxed{V_{\text{bond}} = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} V_{\text{path}\,i}}$$

As $N$ increases, this average converges to the true [[Arbitrage|arbitrage]]-free value.

## The Drift Term

> [!danger] Without the drift, the [[Simulation|simulation]] is NOT [[Arbitrage|arbitrage]]-free!

If you just generate random rate paths, they won't be [[Consistent|consistent]] with today's observed [[Yield curve|yield curve]]. The [[Simulation|simulation]] might imply 2-year bonds should be priced at 99 when the market says 100.

The **drift term** is a correction at each time step that forces the simulated paths to collectively reproduce today's [[Benchmark|benchmark]] bond prices.

| Component | Role |
|-----------|------|
| $r_t$ | Current rate — where we are now |
| $\text{drift}_t$ | Correction ensuring no-[[Arbitrage|arbitrage]] with [[Current yield|current yield]] curve |
| $\sigma \times \varepsilon_t$ | Random shock ([[Normal distribution|normal distribution]]) scaled by [[Volatility|volatility]] |

> [!important] Calibration Is the Same Concept
> **[[Binomial tree|Binomial tree]]:** adjust $R_d$ at each time step until [[Benchmark|benchmark]] bonds price at par
> **Monte Carlo:** adjust the **drift term** at each time step until [[Benchmark|benchmark]] bonds price at par
> 
> Different mechanics, same goal: consistency with the observed [[Term structure|term structure]].

---

## Sampling Error

Unlike the [[Binomial tree|binomial tree]] (which gives an exact answer), Monte Carlo has **[[Sampling error|sampling error]]** — the answer varies slightly each time you run it.

| More paths → | More stable answer | But slower computation |
|:---:|:---:|:---:|

This is the fundamental trade-off of Monte Carlo: accuracy vs. computational cost.

---

## Binomial Tree vs. Monte Carlo

| Feature | [[Binomial tree|Binomial Tree]] | Monte Carlo |
|---------|:-------------:|:-----------:|
| **Best for** | Callable/[[Putable bonds|putable bonds]] | MBS, CMOs, path-[[Dependent|dependent]] securities |
| **Path history** | Lost (recombining) | Preserved (each path tracked) |
| **Early exercise** | ✅ Natural ([[Backward Induction|backward induction]]) | ❌ Difficult |
| **Precision** | Exact | Approximate ([[Sampling error|sampling error]]) |
| **Speed** | Fast | Slow (need many paths) |
| **Calibration** | Adjust $R_d$ | Adjust drift term |

---

## Decision Rule

> [!success] Which method for which bond?
> 
> | Security | Method | Why |
> |----------|--------|-----|
> | Option-free bond | [[Spot rates|Spot rates]] | Simplest |
> | Callable/[[Putable bond|putable bond]] | Binomial tree | [[Backward Induction|Backward induction]] handles exercise decisions |
> | Path-[[Dependent|dependent]] (MBS/CMO) | **Monte Carlo** | Only method preserving full path history |
> 
> All three must be **calibrated to the current [[Term structure|term structure]]** to be [[Arbitrage|arbitrage]]-free.

## Connection to Term Structure Models

The rate evolution equation $r_{t+1} = r_t + \text{drift}_t + \sigma \times \varepsilon_t$ is the discrete version of a continuous-time [[2.08 - Term Structure Models|term structure model]]. The choice of model (CIR, Vasicek, Ho-Lee, KWF) determines the specific form of the drift and [[Volatility|volatility]] terms.
