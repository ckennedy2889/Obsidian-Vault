## Two Families of Models

> [!tip] Analogy
> **[[Equilibrium|Equilibrium]]** models are like predicting weather from atmospheric physics (first principles → forecast).
> **[[Arbitrage|Arbitrage]]-free** models are like starting with today's weather report and modeling how it might change (observed data → evolution).

| Feature | [[Equilibrium|Equilibrium]] Models | [[Arbitrage-free models|Arbitrage-Free Models]] |
|---------|:------------------:|:---------------------:|
| **Philosophy** | Built from economic [[Fundamentals|fundamentals]] | Built from observed market prices |
| **[[Term structure|Term structure]] is…** | An OUTPUT (derived) | An INPUT (given) |
| **Matches today's curve?** | Approximately | Exactly (by construction) |
| **Models** | CIR, Vasicek | Ho-Lee, KWF |

> [!important] For [[Valuation|valuation]] → [[Arbitrage|arbitrage]]ge-free models|[[Arbitrage|arbitrage]]-free models]] (match market prices).
> For economic insight → [[Equilibrium|equilibrium]] models (explain *why* rates behave as they do).

---

## The Common Skeleton

Every model shares the same structure:

$$dr = \underbrace{[\text{drift term}]}_{\text{expected direction}} \times dt + \underbrace{[\text{volatility term}]}_{\text{random noise}} \times dz$$

| Component | Meaning |
|-----------|---------|
| $dr$ | Change in the [[Interest rate|interest rate]] over a tiny time step |
| $dt$ | A tiny slice of time |
| $dz$ | Random shock from a [[Standard normal distribution|standard normal distribution]]on|normal distribution]], scaled by $\sqrt{dt}$ |

---

## The Four Models

### 1. Cox-Ingersoll-Ross (CIR)

$$\boxed{dr = a(b - r)\,dt + \sigma\sqrt{r}\,dz}$$

> [!info] [[Equilibrium|Equilibrium]] model

| Term | What It Does |
|------|-------------|
| $a(b-r)$ | **[[Mean reversion|Mean reversion]]** — pulls rate toward long-run level $b$ at speed $a$. Like a rubber band: the further $r$ drifts from $b$, the harder the pull. |
| $\sigma\sqrt{r}$ | **Level-[[Dependent|dependent]] [[Volatility|volatility]]** — [[Volatility|volatility]] shrinks as $r \to 0$, preventing negative rates. When rates are high, [[Volatility|volatility]] is larger. |

> [!success] Key distinction: $\sigma\sqrt{r}$ means rates **CANNOT go negative**.
> As $r \to 0$: $\sqrt{r} \to 0$ → random shocks vanish → rate stays near zero.

---

### 2. Vasicek

$$\boxed{dr = a(b - r)\,dt + \sigma\,dz}$$

> [!info] Equilibrium model

| Term | What It Does |
|------|-------------|
| $a(b-r)$ | Same [[Mean reversion|mean reversion]] as CIR |
| $\sigma$ | **Constant [[Volatility|volatility]]** — does NOT depend on rate level |

> [!danger] Key distinction: Constant $\sigma$ means rates **CAN go negative**.
> (This was considered a flaw until negative rates actually appeared in Europe/Japan.)

**CIR vs. Vasicek — the only difference:**

| | CIR | Vasicek |
|---|:---:|:-------:|
| Drift | $a(b-r)$ | $a(b-r)$ |
| Volatility | $\sigma\sqrt{r}$ | $\sigma$ |
| Negative rates? | No | Yes |

---

### 3. Ho-Lee

$$\boxed{dr_t = \theta_t\,dt + \sigma\,dz_t}$$

> [!warning] [[Arbitrage|Arbitrage]]-free model

| Term | What It Does |
|------|-------------|
| $\theta_t$ | **Time-varying drift** — changes at every time step, calibrated to match today's [[Yield curve|yield curve]]. No [[Mean reversion|mean reversion]]. |
| $\sigma$ | Constant volatility (same as Vasicek) |

> [!danger] No [[Mean reversion|mean reversion]] → rates can wander indefinitely.
> Constant $\sigma$ → rates **CAN go negative**.

**vs. Vasicek:** Replace the fixed anchor $a(b-r)$ with a time-varying $\theta_t$ that's fitted to market data.

---

### 4. Kalotay-Williams-Fabozzi (KWF)

$$\boxed{d\ln(r_t) = \theta_t\,dt + \sigma\,dz_t}$$

> [!warning] Arbitrage-free model

| Term | What It Does |
|------|-------------|
| $d\ln(r_t)$ | Models the **log of the rate**, not the rate itself |
| $\theta_t$ | Time-varying drift (same as Ho-Lee) |
| $\sigma$ | Constant volatility applied to $\ln(r)$ |

> [!success] Key distinction: Since $r = e^{\ln(r)}$ and $e^{\text{anything}} > 0$, rates **CANNOT go negative**.
> Rates are **lognormally distributed**.

**Connection to the [[Binomial tree|binomial tree]]:**
KWF is the theoretical foundation of the [[2.03 - Binomial Interest Rate Tree|lognormal binomial tree]]:
$$R_u = R_d \times e^{2\sigma\sqrt{t}}$$

## Master Comparison Table

| Feature | CIR | Vasicek | Ho-Lee | KWF |
|---------|:---:|:-------:|:------:|:---:|
| **Type** | Equilibrium | Equilibrium | Arb-free | Arb-free |
| **Formula** | $a(b-r)dt + \sigma\sqrt{r}\,dz$ | $a(b-r)dt + \sigma\,dz$ | $\theta_t dt + \sigma\,dz$ | $d\ln r = \theta_t dt + \sigma\,dz$ |
| **Mean reversion?** | ✅ Yes | ✅ Yes | ❌ No | ❌ No |
| **Negative rates?** | ❌ No ($\sigma\sqrt{r}$) | ⚠️ Yes | ⚠️ Yes | ❌ No (lognormal) |
| **Volatility** | Level-[[Dependent|dependent]] | Constant | Constant | Constant (on $\ln r$) |
| **Matches curve?** | ≈ Approx | ≈ Approx | ✅ Exactly | ✅ Exactly |

## High-Yield Exam Points

> [!important] Memorize These Distinctions
> 
> 1. **CIR vs. Vasicek** — Only difference is $\sigma\sqrt{r}$ (CIR) vs. $\sigma$ (Vasicek) in the volatility term
> 2. **Ho-Lee vs. KWF** — Only difference is KWF models $\ln(r)$ instead of $r$
> 3. **CIR prevents negative rates** because $\sigma\sqrt{r} \to 0$ as $r \to 0$
> 4. **KWF prevents negative rates** because $e^{\text{anything}} > 0$
> 5. **Equilibrium models:** [[Term structure|term structure]] is an OUTPUT
> 6. **[[Arbitrage-free models|Arbitrage-free models]]:** term structure is an INPUT
> 7. **KWF** is the model behind the lognormal [[Binomial tree|binomial tree]] ($R_u = R_d \times e^{2\sigma\sqrt{t}}$)

## The Mean Reversion Concept

For CIR and Vasicek, the drift term $a(b-r)$ acts like a spring:

| If $r$ is… | Then $a(b-r)$ is… | Rate tends to… |
|:---:|:---:|:---:|
| Below $b$ | Positive (+) | Rise back toward $b$ |
| Equal to $b$ | Zero | Stay (no pull) |
| Above $b$ | Negative (−) | Fall back toward $b$ |

**[[Parameter|Parameter]] $a$** = spring stiffness. High $a$ → fast reversion. Low $a$ → slow, lazy drift back.

**[[Parameter|Parameter]] $b$** = long-run equilibrium level. This is where rates "want" to be.

> [!note] Ho-Lee and KWF have NO mean reversion
> Rates can wander freely — the drift $\theta_t$ is purely a calibration device, not an economic anchor.
