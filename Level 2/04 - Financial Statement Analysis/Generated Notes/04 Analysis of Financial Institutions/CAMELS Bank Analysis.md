# CAMELS Bank Analysis

CAMELS is a bank analysis framework that evaluates safety and soundness across six dimensions: capital adequacy, asset quality, management, earnings, liquidity, and sensitivity to market risk.

It is useful because a bank can look profitable while being fragile. CAMELS forces the analyst to ask whether earnings are supported by enough capital, sound assets, stable funding, competent risk management, and manageable market exposures.

## The Six Components

| Letter | Component | Core question |
|---|---|---|
| C | Capital adequacy | Can the bank absorb losses? |
| A | Asset quality | Are loans and investments likely to be collected? |
| M | Management capabilities | Does management control risk and govern well? |
| E | Earnings sufficiency | Are earnings sustainable and adequate? |
| L | Liquidity position | Can the bank meet withdrawals and funding needs? |
| S | Sensitivity to market risk | How exposed is the bank to rates, FX, equity, and commodity movements? |

Ratings typically run from 1 to 5, where 1 is strongest and 5 signals serious weakness.

## C - Capital Adequacy

Capital is the bank's loss cushion.

Basel-style capital ratios compare capital to [[Risk-weighted assets]] rather than total assets.

| Ratio | Formula | Basel III minimum |
|---|---|---:|
| CET1 ratio | Common Equity Tier 1 / RWA | 4.5% |
| Tier 1 ratio | Tier 1 capital / RWA | 6.0% |
| Total capital ratio | Total capital / RWA | 8.0% |

Higher capital lowers failure risk, but too much capital may reduce [[ROE]] if the bank cannot deploy it profitably.

## A - Asset Quality

Asset quality focuses mainly on loan credit risk.

Important signals:

- Non-performing loans as a percentage of total loans.
- Allowance for loan losses relative to total loans or non-performing loans.
- Loan loss provisions relative to loan growth and charge-offs.
- Concentration by borrower, sector, or geography.
- Level 3 fair value assets as a percentage of assets.

Rising non-performing loans with flat or declining allowances is a warning sign.

## M - Management Capabilities

Management quality is qualitative but critical.

Evidence includes:

- Governance and board independence.
- Internal controls.
- Risk management systems.
- Regulatory history.
- Restatements or audit issues.
- Risk appetite and incentive design.

Weak management can destroy a bank even when starting ratios look strong.

## E - Earnings

Bank earnings quality depends on source and sustainability.

| Revenue source | Quality tendency |
|---|---|
| Net interest income | Core and relatively stable |
| Fee and service income | Often recurring, but business dependent |
| Trading gains | More volatile |

Analysts watch [[Net interest margin]], provision behavior, one-time gains, and reliance on model-based fair value estimates.

## L - Liquidity

Liquidity asks whether the bank can meet cash outflows under stress.

Signals include:

- Stable deposit base.
- Liquid securities portfolio.
- Wholesale funding dependence.
- Maturity mismatch.
- Liquidity coverage metrics.

A solvent bank can still fail if it cannot fund itself.

## S - Sensitivity to Market Risk

Sensitivity measures exposure to movements in:

- Interest rates.
- Foreign exchange rates.
- Equity prices.
- Commodity prices.
- Credit spreads.

For banks, interest rate risk is often central because assets and liabilities reprice at different speeds.

## Limitations

CAMELS is a framework, not a formula. It requires judgment and can lag fast-moving stress.

Limitations:

- Public analysts may not have supervisory information.
- Some components are qualitative.
- Accounting estimates can obscure asset quality.
- Strong scores in one area do not offset fatal weakness in another.
- The rating is not a valuation model by itself.

## Exam Traps

| Trap | Correct interpretation |
|---|---|
| "CAMELS is only about capital ratios." | False. Capital is one of six components. |
| "A profitable bank is automatically safe." | False. Earnings may come from risky assets or under-provisioning. |
| "Liquidity and solvency are the same." | False. A bank can be solvent but illiquid. |
| "C comes first, so it is always most important." | False. The order is mnemonic, not a ranking. |

## Memory Hook

CAMELS asks: "Can the bank survive losses, funding pressure, bad management, weak earnings, and market shocks?"
