# Combined Ratio and CRAD

The combined ratio is the core underwriting profitability metric for property and casualty insurers. It asks whether premiums earned from underwriting are enough to cover claims and underwriting expenses before considering investment income.

An insurer can report positive net income with a combined ratio above 100% if investment income is strong. But underwriting itself is losing money.

## Premium Terminology

| Term | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Direct premiums written | Premiums written before reinsurance |
| Net premiums written | Direct premiums written minus premiums ceded to reinsurers |
| Net premiums earned | Premiums recognized as revenue for coverage provided during the period |

Premiums are often billed before coverage is fully provided. Written premiums measure business volume; earned premiums match coverage delivered.

## Loss Ratio

$$
\text{Loss ratio}
= \frac{\text{Loss expense} + \text{Loss adjustment expense}}
{\text{Net premiums earned}}
$$

The loss ratio measures underwriting quality. Lower is better because fewer premium dollars are consumed by claims and claim handling.

## Underwriting Expense Ratio

$$
\text{Expense ratio}
= \frac{\text{Underwriting expenses}}
{\text{Net premiums written}}
$$

The expense ratio measures operating efficiency in acquiring and servicing policies.

Some companies report this ratio using net premiums earned instead of written, so analysts must check the denominator before comparing firms.

## Combined Ratio

$$
\text{Combined ratio}
= \text{Loss ratio} + \text{Expense ratio}
$$

| Combined ratio | Interpretation |
|---:|---|
| Below 100% | Underwriting profit |
| Equal to 100% | Underwriting break-even |
| Above 100% | Underwriting loss |

The lower the combined ratio, the more profitable the underwriting operation.

## Dividends Ratio and CRAD

Some insurers pay dividends to policyholders.

$$
\text{Dividends ratio}
= \frac{\text{Dividends to policyholders}}
{\text{Net premiums earned}}
$$

Combined ratio after dividends:

$$
\text{CRAD}
= \text{Combined ratio} + \text{Dividends ratio}
$$

CRAD is stricter because it includes policyholder distributions after underwriting expenses and losses.

## Worked Example

An insurer reports:

- Net premiums earned: 1,000.
- Net premiums written: 1,100.
- Loss and loss adjustment expenses: 620.
- Underwriting expenses: 330.
- Policyholder dividends: 20.

Loss ratio:

$$
\frac{620}{1{,}000} = 62.0\%
$$

Expense ratio:

$$
\frac{330}{1{,}100} = 30.0\%
$$

Combined ratio:

$$
62.0\% + 30.0\% = 92.0\%
$$

Dividends ratio:

$$
\frac{20}{1{,}000} = 2.0\%
$$

CRAD:

$$
92.0\% + 2.0\% = 94.0\%
$$

The insurer has underwriting profit even after policyholder dividends.

## Underwriting Cycle

The combined ratio also helps explain the property and casualty underwriting cycle.

| Market | Typical behavior | Combined ratio pressure |
|---|---|---|
| Hard market | Tight underwriting, higher premiums | Lower combined ratio |
| Soft market | Price competition, looser underwriting | Higher combined ratio |

Profitable underwriting attracts competition. Competition cuts pricing and weakens underwriting discipline. Eventually poor profitability pushes weak insurers out, and pricing hardens again.

## Exam Traps

| Trap | Correct interpretation |
|---|---|
| "A combined ratio above 100% means the insurer has a net loss." | False. Investment income can still make total net income positive. |
| "Lower combined ratio is worse." | False. Lower means better underwriting profitability. |
| "Expense ratio always uses net premiums earned." | Not always. Many formulas use net premiums written; check the question. |
| "CRAD equals combined ratio minus dividends." | False. CRAD adds the dividends ratio. |

## Memory Hook

Combined ratio asks: "Did underwriting pay for claims and expenses?"

CRAD asks: "Did underwriting pay for claims, expenses, and policyholder dividends?"
