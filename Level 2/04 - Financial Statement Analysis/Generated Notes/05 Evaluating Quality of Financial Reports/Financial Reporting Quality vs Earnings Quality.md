# Financial Reporting Quality vs Earnings Quality

Financial reporting quality and earnings quality are related, but they are not the same.

Financial reporting quality asks whether the financial statements are reliable and useful. Earnings quality asks whether the company's actual economic results are sustainable and adequate.

## The Two Questions

| Dimension | Core question | High quality means |
|---|---|---|
| Financial reporting quality | Can I trust the reported information? | Relevant, complete, neutral, and free from material error |
| Earnings quality | Are the economics good? | Earnings are sustainable and earn more than the cost of capital |

The dependency matters: you cannot assess earnings quality well if reporting quality is poor. If the numbers are manipulated, it does not matter how attractive the margins or ROE appear.

## Four-Quadrant View

| | High earnings quality | Low earnings quality |
|---|---|---|
| High reporting quality | Strong economics reported faithfully | Weak or unsustainable economics reported honestly |
| Low reporting quality | Possibly good business, but numbers are unreliable | Worst case: weak economics plus unreliable reporting |

The exam often tests this distinction. A company can have high reporting quality but low earnings quality if it honestly reports declining, nonrecurring, or inadequate results.

## Financial Reporting Quality

High-quality financial reporting has two broad characteristics:

- Relevance: information affects decisions and is material.
- Faithful representation: information is complete, neutral, and free from material error.

Low reporting quality can come from:

- Biased estimates.
- Omitted liabilities.
- Aggressive revenue recognition.
- Classification choices that obscure performance.
- Fraudulent reporting.

## Earnings Quality

High-quality earnings are:

- Persistent.
- Cash-supported.
- Recurring.
- Adequate relative to required return.
- Not dependent on one-time gains, accounting estimates, or temporary cost cuts.

An important benchmark is whether [[ROIC]] exceeds [[WACC]]. A company can report positive net income but still have low earnings quality if it fails to cover its cost of capital.

## Common Warning Signs

| Warning sign | Why it matters |
|---|---|
| Net income persistently exceeds CFO | Accruals may be aggressive |
| Receivables grow faster than revenue | Revenue recognition may be stretched |
| Inventory grows faster than sales | Obsolescence or demand issues may be hidden |
| Large nonrecurring gains | Earnings may not persist |
| Frequent "one-time" charges | Management may be normalizing recurring problems away |
| Complex related-party transactions | Substance may differ from form |
| Executive compensation tied tightly to accounting targets | Incentive to manage earnings |

## Analyst Workflow

1. Test reporting quality first.
2. Normalize earnings for unusual and nonrecurring items.
3. Compare net income with operating cash flow.
4. Examine accruals.
5. Evaluate sustainability of margins, growth, and returns.
6. Compare returns with cost of capital.

## Exam Traps

| Trap | Correct interpretation |
|---|---|
| "Honest reporting means high-quality earnings." | False. The company may honestly report poor or unsustainable results. |
| "High earnings quality means reporting quality is automatically high." | Not necessarily. Apparent quality can be an artifact of bad reporting. |
| "Positive net income means adequate earnings." | False. Earnings must be judged relative to capital employed and cost of capital. |
| "One-time gains improve earnings quality." | Usually false. They increase current income but reduce persistence. |

## Memory Hook

Reporting quality asks: "Are the numbers trustworthy?"

Earnings quality asks: "Are the underlying economics worth owning?"
