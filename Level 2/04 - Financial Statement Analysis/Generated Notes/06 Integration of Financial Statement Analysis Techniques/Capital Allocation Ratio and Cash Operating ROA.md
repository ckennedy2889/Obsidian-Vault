# Capital Allocation Ratio and Cash Operating ROA

Segment analysis asks whether management is putting capital into the right businesses. Consolidated statements can hide this because strong divisions and weak divisions are blended together.

The capital allocation ratio compares where a company is spending new capital with where its existing asset base sits.

## Capital Allocation Ratio

$$
\text{Capital allocation ratio}
= \frac{\text{Segment's percentage of total CapEx}}
{\text{Segment's percentage of total assets}}
$$

| Ratio | Interpretation |
|---:|---|
| 1.0 | Segment receives capital spending proportional to its asset base |
| Greater than 1.0 | Segment is receiving a growth allocation |
| Less than 1.0 | Segment is being under-invested relative to current size |

This ratio becomes useful when compared with segment profitability.

## What the Ratio Reveals

| Segment pattern | Interpretation |
|---|---|
| High margins and ratio above 1.0 | Management is investing in a strong segment |
| Low margins and ratio above 1.0 | Possible capital misallocation |
| High margins and ratio below 1.0 | Strong segment may be starved of capital |
| Low margins and ratio below 1.0 | Management may be shrinking a weak segment |

The exam will often give segment margins, assets, and capital expenditures and ask whether capital is being allocated well.

## Worked Example

| Segment | EBIT margin | Percent of assets | Percent of CapEx | Capital allocation ratio |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| Aircraft | 22.0% | 18.5% | 12.3% | 0.66 |
| Automotive | 12.5% | 42.1% | 43.7% | 1.04 |
| Marine | 10.8% | 26.2% | 24.8% | 0.95 |
| Specialty | 5.2% | 13.2% | 19.2% | 1.45 |

Specialty has the lowest EBIT margin but the highest capital allocation ratio. That suggests management may be overallocating resources to the weakest segment.

Aircraft has the highest EBIT margin but a ratio below 1.0, suggesting it may be underfunded relative to its profitability.

## Cash Operating Return on Assets

EBIT margin can be misleading because it ignores the asset base required to generate the margin. A segment can have a high margin but poor asset productivity if acquisitions created large goodwill and intangible assets.

Cash operating return on assets:

$$
\text{Cash operating ROA}
= \frac{EBIT + \text{Depreciation and amortization}}
{\text{Average total assets}}
$$

Adding back depreciation and amortization approximates cash operating profit before noncash charges. Dividing by assets shows how much cash operating return the segment generates from its asset base.

## Why Cash Operating ROA Can Change the Story

| Metric | What it emphasizes | Blind spot |
|---|---|---|
| EBIT margin | Profit per sales dollar | Ignores asset intensity |
| Cash operating ROA | Cash operating return on asset base | Still affected by asset measurement and acquisitions |

A segment with high EBIT margin but low cash operating ROA may look profitable only because the margin denominator is sales, while the business actually ties up a large amount of capital.

## Analyst Implications

Use these metrics together:

1. Identify the most and least profitable segments.
2. Check whether CapEx is going to high-return segments.
3. Compare EBIT margin with cash operating ROA.
4. Watch for goodwill-heavy acquired segments with weak asset returns.
5. Question management strategy when capital flows to low-return segments.

## Exam Traps

| Trap | Correct interpretation |
|---|---|
| "A capital allocation ratio above 1.0 is always good." | Not if the segment has low returns. |
| "The highest EBIT margin segment is always the best capital user." | Not necessarily. Check asset intensity and cash operating ROA. |
| "Consolidated ROA tells you where capital is being allocated." | False. Segment analysis is needed. |
| "Cash operating ROA subtracts depreciation and amortization." | False. It adds them back to EBIT. |

## Memory Hook

Capital allocation ratio asks: "Where is the company sending the next dollar?"

Cash operating ROA asks: "What cash return is each asset dollar producing?"
