# Acquisition Method

## The Real-World Analogy

If the [[Equity Method]] is "I influence the company," the [[Acquisition Method]] is "I control the company."

When a parent controls a subsidiary, the financial statements are prepared as if the parent and subsidiary are one economic entity.

```text
Control
  ->
Combine assets, liabilities, revenues, and expenses
  ->
Show noncontrolling interest for the part not owned
```

## Plain-English Definition

The acquisition method is used for business combinations when one company controls another.

Under the acquisition method, the parent consolidates:

| Item | Treatment |
|---|---|
| Subsidiary assets | 100% included |
| Subsidiary liabilities | 100% included |
| Subsidiary revenues | 100% included |
| Subsidiary expenses | 100% included |
| Portion not owned | Reported as [[Noncontrolling interest]] |
| Intercompany transactions | Eliminated |

Control, not percent ownership alone, drives consolidation.

## Why CFA Tests This

CFA tests the acquisition method because it changes financial statements and ratios dramatically compared with the equity method.

Key idea:

```text
Acquisition method grosses up the statements.
Equity method keeps the investee in one line.
```

This affects leverage, margins, asset turnover, and comparability.

## Balance Sheet Mechanics

At acquisition, the acquirer recognizes the acquiree's identifiable assets and liabilities at fair value.

If the purchase price exceeds the fair value of identifiable net assets acquired, the excess is [[Goodwill]].

For less than 100% ownership, the unowned share is reported as noncontrolling interest.

Simplified:

$$
\text{Goodwill}
=\text{Consideration paid}
+\text{Fair value of NCI}
-\text{Fair value of identifiable net assets}
$$

In many simplified exam settings:

$$
\text{NCI}=\text{Subsidiary equity}\times\text{unowned \%}
$$

## Income Statement Mechanics

The parent reports 100% of the subsidiary's revenues and expenses.

Then it subtracts the portion of subsidiary income attributable to noncontrolling shareholders.

$$
\text{NCI share of income}
=\text{Subsidiary NI}\times\text{unowned \%}
$$

Important result:

```text
Parent shareholders' net income can be the same under equity method and acquisition method,
but revenue, expenses, assets, liabilities, and ratios can differ substantially.
```

## Worked Example

Parent owns 80% of Subsidiary. Subsidiary reports:

| Item | Amount |
|---|---:|
| Revenue | 10,000 |
| Expenses | 6,000 |
| Net income | 4,000 |

Under acquisition accounting, consolidated statements include:

| Item | Consolidated effect |
|---|---:|
| Revenue | +10,000 |
| Expenses | +6,000 |
| Subsidiary NI before NCI | +4,000 |
| Less NCI share | $4{,}000 \times 20\% = 800$ |
| Net income attributable to parent | 3,200 |

Under the equity method, the parent would show one line:

$$
\text{Equity income}=4{,}000\times80\%=3{,}200
$$

Same parent-level income, but very different statements.

## Ratio Effects vs Equity Method

| Ratio / item | Acquisition method compared with equity method |
|---|---|
| Revenue | Higher |
| Expenses | Higher |
| Assets | Higher |
| Liabilities | Higher |
| Net income to parent | Usually same |
| Net profit margin | Often lower because revenue is grossed up |
| Leverage | Often higher because subsidiary liabilities are included |
| Asset turnover | Can change materially because assets and revenue are grossed up |

Analyst reflex:

```text
Acquisition method shows more economic leverage.
Equity method can hide debt in associates.
```

## Intercompany Eliminations

Because consolidated statements treat the parent and subsidiary as one entity, transactions between them cannot create external revenue or profit.

Common eliminations:

| Item | Why eliminated |
|---|---|
| Intercompany sales | The group cannot sell to itself |
| Intercompany receivables/payables | The group cannot owe itself |
| Intercompany profit in inventory | Profit is not confirmed until sold outside the group |
| Intercompany dividends | Internal distributions are not income to the group |

## Exam Traps

| Trap | Correct reflex |
|---|---|
| Consolidating only ownership percentage | Acquisition method includes 100% of subsidiary assets/liabilities/revenue/expenses |
| Forgetting noncontrolling interest | Report the unowned share in equity and income allocation |
| Thinking net income must differ from equity method | Parent-level NI can be the same even when statements are grossed up |
| Forgetting intercompany eliminations | Consolidated group cannot transact with itself |
| Confusing acquisition method with purchase method history | Current IFRS and US GAAP require acquisition method for business combinations |

## Memory Hook

```text
Control means consolidate.
Consolidate means 100% in, then subtract NCI.
```
