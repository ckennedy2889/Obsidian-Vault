# SPE VIE Consolidation

A [[Special purpose entity]] is a legally separate entity created for a narrow purpose, such as securitizing receivables, leasing assets, or financing a project. A [[Variable interest entity]] is the US GAAP framing for an entity where control is driven by economic exposure rather than ordinary voting rights.

The exam idea is control without voting. A sponsor may own little voting equity but still bear the real risk and receive the real rewards. If so, the sponsor should consolidate the entity.

## Why SPEs Exist

SPEs can serve legitimate purposes:

- Isolating assets for securitization.
- Lowering financing costs.
- Separating project risk.
- Creating legal bankruptcy remoteness.

But they can also be used to hide liabilities. If a company transfers receivables or assets into an SPE, receives cash, and keeps the SPE debt off its balance sheet, leverage may appear lower even though the company still bears the economics.

## Economic Substance

The analyst asks:

> Who controls the relevant activities, absorbs expected losses, or receives expected residual returns?

If the sponsor is the real risk bearer, the SPE is not economically independent.

## IFRS Control Model

Under IFRS, the control definition captures structured entities.

Control exists when the investor has:

1. Power over relevant activities.
2. Exposure or rights to variable returns.
3. Ability to use power to affect those returns.

If these conditions are met, the sponsor consolidates the entity.

## US GAAP VIE Model

Under US GAAP, an entity may be a VIE when equity investors do not have the normal characteristics of real equity capital.

Common indicators:

- Equity at risk is insufficient to finance operations without additional support.
- Equity investors lack decision-making power.
- Equity investors do not absorb expected losses.
- Equity investors do not receive expected residual returns.

The primary beneficiary consolidates the VIE. The primary beneficiary is the party with power over significant activities and exposure to potentially significant benefits or losses.

## Worked Example

Company P wants financing secured by receivables.

| Direct borrowing | SPE structure before consolidation |
|---|---|
| Borrow cash and record debt | Sell receivables to SPE and receive cash |
| Debt appears on P's balance sheet | SPE debt may appear separate |
| Leverage rises visibly | Leverage can look artificially low |

If P guarantees the SPE's debt and controls the receivables program, consolidation adds the SPE's assets and liabilities back to P's statements. After consolidation, the economics resemble direct borrowing.

## Analyst Implications

SPE/VIE structures affect quality of reporting because they can compromise balance sheet completeness.

Watch for:

- Guarantees of SPE debt.
- Retained interests in transferred assets.
- Thin third-party equity.
- Related-party support.
- Large securitization programs.
- Operating leases or financing structures that shift assets and debt away from the sponsor.

The key adjustment is to bring hidden assets and liabilities back into the analysis when the sponsor bears the economics.

## Exam Traps

| Trap | Correct interpretation |
|---|---|
| "No voting control means no consolidation." | False. Economic control can require consolidation. |
| "An SPE is always abusive." | False. It may be legitimate, but analysts must test substance over form. |
| "Selling assets to an SPE always removes risk." | False. Guarantees, recourse, and retained interests can keep risk with the sponsor. |
| "Equity at risk always means real equity." | Not if the equity is too thin or lacks genuine risk/return characteristics. |

## Memory Hook

Do not ask only, "Who owns the voting shares?"

Ask, "Who cannot walk away from the economics?"
