# Cointegration

## The Drunk and His Dog — An Analogy First

Picture a drunk man stumbling home from a bar. His path is unpredictable: each step is a random nudge in some direction, and if you ask "where will he be in ten minutes?" the honest answer is "somewhere, but I have no idea where." He is a **random walk** — what statisticians call a [[Unit Root]] process, or *integrated of order one*, written $I(1)$.

Now imagine he has a dog with him, also drunk in spirit, also wandering. The dog, on its own, is *also* a random walk. If you tracked the dog independently, you couldn't predict its location either.

Here is the magic. **Suppose the drunk and his dog are connected by a leash.** Each one wanders unpredictably, but the *distance between them* can never grow beyond the length of the leash. Whenever the gap stretches, the leash tugs them back together. Their individual positions are unstable forever, but the **gap between their positions is stable** — it hovers around a constant mean (the leash's natural slack).

That stable gap is what cointegration is about. Two non-stationary economic series — say, [[GDP]] and corporate sales, or two stock prices in the same industry, or short and long interest rates — can each wander like a random walk, yet be tied together by a **long-run economic equilibrium**. The equilibrium is the leash. The "gap" between them, which we measure as the **residuals of a regression of one on the other**, is stationary. That is the formal signature of cointegration.

Why does this matter? Because if you don't check for it, regressing one wandering series on another is a statistical disaster — the so-called **spurious regression** problem. Cointegration is the test that tells you whether your regression is real or a mirage.

---

## The Problem Cointegration Solves: Spurious Regression

To understand *why* CFA cares about cointegration, you must first understand the trap it rescues you from.

Ordinary Least Squares regression — the workhorse of every analyst — assumes the variables involved are **stationary**: their mean, variance, and autocovariance don't change with time. Most macro and financial variables in *levels* (price, GDP, sales, money supply) violate this assumption flagrantly. They have trends. They drift. They are $I(1)$.

So what happens if you regress two trending, non-stationary series on each other anyway? The computer happily produces output. And here is the cruel part: **the output looks fantastic**. You will see:

- An R² near 0.95
- t-statistics in the double digits
- An F-statistic that screams "significant"

Every diagnostic tells you that you have discovered a brilliant relationship. **It is a lie.** The high R² is an artifact of two series sharing a common trend (both going up over time, for instance), not an artifact of any genuine economic linkage. Granger and Newbold famously showed in 1974 that you can take *two completely unrelated* random walks — generated by independent random number generators — regress one on the other, and the regression will look "significant" the majority of the time. That is the **spurious regression** problem.

> **Why:** The regression's standard errors are wrong because the residuals are themselves non-stationary. The classical inference machinery breaks down silently.

So we need a gatekeeper. Before we trust *any* regression on level (raw, undifferenced) non-stationary data, we must ask: **are the variables cointegrated?** Cointegration is the certificate that says "yes, the leash is real, the long-run relationship is genuine, and OLS on the levels is salvageable."

---

## Formal Definition

Two time series $X_t$ and $Y_t$ are **cointegrated** if and only if both of the following hold:

1. **Each series individually has a unit root.** That is, both $X_t$ and $Y_t$ are $I(1)$ — non-stationary in levels, but stationary after first-differencing ($\Delta X_t = X_t - X_{t-1}$ is $I(0)$, likewise for $Y_t$).
2. **A linear combination of them is stationary.** Specifically, in the regression
$$Y_t = b_0 + b_1 X_t + \varepsilon_t$$
the residuals $\varepsilon_t$ are $I(0)$ (stationary).

Notation: if $X_t \sim I(1)$ and $Y_t \sim I(1)$ but $\varepsilon_t \sim I(0)$, we say $X_t$ and $Y_t$ are $CI(1,1)$ — *cointegrated of order one, one*.

The economic interpretation: although both series wander forever, they cannot wander *independently*. They are tethered. Whenever the residual $\varepsilon_t$ — the deviation from the long-run relationship — gets large, mean-reversion drags it back. The leash tugs.

> **Why this passes the spurious test:** if $\varepsilon_t$ is stationary, the OLS standard errors *do* converge to sensible values, and inference on $b_1$ becomes valid (asymptotically). The relationship is not a coincidence of trends; it is a real equilibrium.

## The Engle–Granger Test — Step by Step

The Engle–Granger procedure is the workhorse cointegration test on the CFA curriculum. It has three steps. Memorize the order.

### Step 1 — Establish that each series is $I(1)$

Run a [[Dickey-Fuller Test]] (typically the augmented version, [[ADF Test]]) on $X_t$ and on $Y_t$ separately.

- $H_0$: the series has a unit root (non-stationary).
- $H_1$: the series is stationary.

You want to **fail to reject** $H_0$ for both — confirming both series are non-stationary in levels. Then run the same test on the *first differences* $\Delta X_t$ and $\Delta Y_t$. You want to **reject** $H_0$ this time, confirming both are stationary after one difference. That establishes both as $I(1)$.

### Step 2 — Estimate the cointegrating regression on levels

$$Y_t = b_0 + b_1 X_t + \varepsilon_t$$

Use raw, undifferenced data. Save the residuals $\hat{\varepsilon}_t = Y_t - \hat{b}_0 - \hat{b}_1 X_t$.

### Step 3 — Test the residuals for a unit root

Run a Dickey–Fuller test on $\hat{\varepsilon}_t$.

- $H_0$: the residuals have a unit root (so $X$ and $Y$ are **NOT** cointegrated).
- $H_1$: the residuals are stationary (so $X$ and $Y$ **ARE** cointegrated).

**Critical caveat:** you cannot use the standard Dickey–Fuller critical values here. Because the residuals are themselves estimated (you used OLS to construct them in Step 2), the distribution shifts. You must use the **Engle–Granger critical values**, which are *more negative* (stricter) than the standard DF values. Failing to use the right table is a classic exam trap.

---

## Interpreting the Result — and Choosing the Right Model

The whole point of testing for cointegration is to answer the question: *what regression am I allowed to run?* The answer depends entirely on the result.

| Scenario | Both series in levels | Cointegrated? | What to model | Why |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **A** | Both $I(0)$ stationary | N/A | OLS on levels — go ahead | Standard regression assumptions hold |
| **B** | Both $I(1)$, **cointegrated** | Yes | OLS on levels is **valid** — captures the long-run equilibrium | Residuals are $I(0)$, so inference works |
| **C** | Both $I(1)$, **NOT cointegrated** | No | **Do NOT** use levels. Use first-differences: $\Delta Y_t = b_0 + b_1 \Delta X_t + u_t$ | Differencing makes each side $I(0)$, restoring stationarity |
| **D** | One $I(0)$, one $I(1)$ (mixed orders) | Impossible | Regression is **invalid** in either form | Error term inherits non-stationarity from the $I(1)$ side |

The four-case table is the LOS-level summary you need at exam time. Notice scenario D: if your two series have *different* orders of integration, no transformation in the standard toolkit saves you — that regression is broken.

## Worked Numerical Example — Intel Sales vs. GDP

An analyst suspects that Intel's quarterly sales depend on the size of the U.S. economy. She has 25 years (100 quarters) of data on $\ln(\text{Intel Sales})_t$ and $\ln(\text{GDP})_t$. Both look trending and non-stationary in their plots. She runs the Engle–Granger procedure.

**Step 1 — Unit root tests on each series.**
The ADF t-statistic on $\ln(\text{Sales})$ is $-1.42$; on $\ln(\text{GDP})$ it is $-1.10$. The 5% Dickey–Fuller critical value is approximately $-2.89$. Both t-stats are *less negative* than the critical value, so she **fails to reject** $H_0$ — both have a unit root. After first-differencing, both ADF t-stats become highly negative (e.g., $-6.5$), so the differenced series are stationary. Conclusion: both raw series are $I(1)$. ✓

**Step 2 — Cointegrating regression on levels.**
$$\ln(\text{Sales})_t = b_0 + b_1 \ln(\text{GDP})_t + \varepsilon_t$$
She estimates $\hat{b}_0 = -3.21$, $\hat{b}_1 = 1.84$, with $R^2 = 0.97$. The R² is suspiciously high — but she knows not to trust it yet. She extracts the residuals $\hat{\varepsilon}_t$.

**Step 3 — Dickey–Fuller test on the residuals.**
The DF t-statistic on $\hat{\varepsilon}_t$ is $-4.20$. The **Engle–Granger** 5% critical value (not the standard DF value!) is $-3.39$.

Decision rule: reject $H_0$ if test statistic $<$ critical value (more negative).

$$-4.20 \;<\; -3.39 \quad\Rightarrow\quad \text{Reject } H_0$$

**Conclusion.** The residuals are stationary. Intel sales and GDP are cointegrated. The estimated relationship $\ln(\text{Sales}) = -3.21 + 1.84 \cdot \ln(\text{GDP})$ describes a **genuine long-run equilibrium**: a 1% rise in GDP is associated, in the long run, with a 1.84% rise in Intel sales. The regression is **not** spurious. The R² of 0.97 is now trustworthy as a description of long-run co-movement.

Had the residual t-stat been, say, $-2.50$, she would have *failed* to reject — concluded NO cointegration — and been forced to abandon the levels regression. She would instead model $\Delta \ln(\text{Sales})_t$ on $\Delta \ln(\text{GDP})_t$, capturing only short-term quarterly co-movement.

## Connection to Unit Roots and Dickey–Fuller Testing

It helps to see cointegration as the *next layer* of a hierarchy of stationarity tests:

```
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│ Layer 1: Is one series stationary?                          │
│   Tool: Dickey–Fuller / Augmented Dickey–Fuller             │
│   Critical values: standard DF table                        │
├─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│ Layer 2: Are two non-stationary series tethered?            │
│   Tool: Engle–Granger (DF applied to OLS residuals)         │
│   Critical values: Engle–Granger table (stricter)           │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

The reason the Engle–Granger critical values are stricter: in Layer 1 you test *observed* data; in Layer 2 you test *estimated residuals*. The estimation in Step 2 introduces extra sampling variability, which makes spurious "stationarity" of residuals more likely under the null. The critical value table compensates by demanding a more extreme t-statistic before declaring stationarity.

## Exam Pitfalls — Read These Twice

1. **Wrong critical value table.** Using standard Dickey–Fuller critical values to test residuals for cointegration. Always use the Engle–Granger table — it is stricter (more negative).
2. **Skipping Step 1.** If you don't first verify both series are $I(1)$, the whole Engle–Granger framework collapses. Cointegration only makes sense between same-order non-stationary series.
3. **Mixed orders of integration.** Regressing an $I(0)$ on an $I(1)$ (or vice versa) is invalid in levels *and* in differences. There is no rescue with the basic toolkit.
4. **Trusting R² and t-stats blindly.** A high R² on level data is *evidence of nothing* until you've established cointegration. It is the very lure of the spurious regression trap.
5. **Confusing short-run and long-run.** A cointegrated levels regression captures the long-run equilibrium beautifully but can perform poorly for short-horizon forecasting, where short-term dynamics dominate.
6. **Standard t-tables for unit root tests.** The DF distribution is non-standard. Never compare a DF t-stat to a standard normal or t-table critical value.
7. **Forgetting the sign convention.** DF and Engle–Granger critical values are *negative*. You reject when your t-stat is *more negative* than the critical value, not less.

## LOS-Level Recap

| LOS Verb | Mastery Statement |
|---|---|
| **Describe** cointegration | Two $I(1)$ series whose residuals from an OLS regression in levels are $I(0)$ — i.e., they share a stable long-run equilibrium relationship. |
| **Explain** the implication for regression | If cointegrated, OLS on levels is valid and captures the long-run relationship; if not cointegrated, levels regression is spurious and you must regress on first differences instead. |
| **Distinguish** outcomes via Engle–Granger | Run DF on each series (both must be $I(1)$); regress $Y$ on $X$ in levels; run DF on residuals using **Engle–Granger** critical values; reject $H_0$ ⇒ cointegrated; fail to reject ⇒ not cointegrated, switch to differences. |
| **Calculate / interpret** the test | Compare residual DF t-statistic to Engle–Granger critical value; reject if test stat is more negative than critical value. |
| **Compare** to the alternatives | Vs. unit root test (single series); vs. spurious regression (the failure mode); vs. first-difference regression (the fallback when no cointegration). |

---

## One-Sentence Summary

Cointegration is the leash that makes two random walks tell the truth about each other — and the Engle–Granger test is how you check whether the leash is really there before you trust any regression on the raw data.
