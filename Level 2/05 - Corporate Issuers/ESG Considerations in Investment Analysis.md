# ESG Considerations in Investment Analysis — CFA Level II

> **Module 2 | Corporate Issuers | CFA Level II**
> *Why do investors care about how a company treats the planet, its people, and its own boardroom?* Because mismanaging any of these can blow up a stock price overnight.

---

## Why This Module Matters

Think of [[ESG]] as a **health check-up for a company beyond just its financial statements**. A company might look great on paper — strong revenue, solid margins — but if it's dumping toxic waste, underpaying workers, or has a board full of the CEO's golf buddies, there are hidden risks that can crush shareholder value.

**Real-world example:** In 2010, BP's Deepwater Horizon oil spill wiped out roughly **\$100 billion** in market capitalization. The financials looked fine *before* the spill. ESG analysis might have flagged the environmental risk.

## Section 1: Ownership Structures and Their Effects on Corporate Governance

### 1.1 The Big Picture: Who Owns the Company?

Before we can evaluate how well a company is governed, we need to understand **who actually controls it**. The [[ownership structure]] of a company determines the power dynamics — who calls the shots, who gets ignored, and where conflicts arise.

> **Analogy:** Think of a company like a school. In some schools, every parent has an equal say at PTA meetings ([[dispersed ownership]]). In others, one wealthy donor funds most of the school and basically decides everything ([[concentrated ownership]]). The governance challenges are completely different in each case.

### 1.2 Dispersed vs. Concentrated Ownership

#### [[Dispersed Ownership]]

**Definition:** Many [[shareholders]] own the company, but **no single shareholder (or group) has enough shares to control it**.

- Common in the **US** and **UK**
- Each shareholder owns a tiny slice — think of how millions of people own shares of Apple
- **Key problem:** No single owner has the incentive or power to keep management in check

#### [[Concentrated Ownership]]

**Definition:** One shareholder or a small group of shareholders holds **enough shares to exercise control** over the [[Corporation|corporation]].

- Common in **Latin America, Asia**, and parts of **Europe**
- The controlling shareholder might be a family, a government, or another [[Corporation|corporation]]
- **Key problem:** The controlling shareholder can use the company for their own benefit at the expense of smaller investors

#### [[Hybrid Ownership]]

A mix of both — some shareholders have significant stakes, but there's also a large public float. This is actually the most common structure globally.

### 1.3 How Ownership Creates Control: Special Structures

#### [[Horizontal Ownership]] (Cross-Holdings)

**Definition:** Companies with mutual business interests hold shares *in each other*.

> **Analogy:** Imagine three friends who each own a piece of each other's businesses. Friend A owns stock in B and C, B owns stock in A and C, and so on. They form a web of loyalty — and outsiders can't easily break in.

**Real-world example:** Japanese [[keiretsu]] — Mitsubishi companies (bank, motors, chemicals) historically held shares in each other, creating a tight network that was nearly impossible for outsiders to penetrate.

#### [[Vertical Ownership]] (Pyramid Structure)

**Definition:** A parent company or group controls [[holding companies]], which in turn control [[operating companies]], stacking control downward like a pyramid.

> **Analogy:** Picture a Russian nesting doll. The outermost doll (parent company) controls the next one inside (holding company), which controls the smallest one (operating company). The person at the top controls everything with relatively little actual money invested.

**Why it matters:** A family might own 51% of Company A, which owns 51% of Company B, which owns 51% of Company C. The family effectively controls Company C while only owning about **13%** of its total economic value (0.51 × 0.51 × 0.51 ≈ 0.13).

#### [[Dual-Class Shares]] (Multiple-Class Shares)

**Definition:** A share structure where **one class of shares gets superior voting rights** and another class gets inferior (or no) voting rights.

- Company founders keep the high-voting shares (e.g., 10 votes per share)
- Public investors get the low-voting shares (e.g., 1 vote per share)
- The founder keeps control even as they sell most of the economic ownership

**Real-world example:** Google (Alphabet) has Class A (1 vote), Class B (10 votes, held by founders), and Class C (0 votes). Larry Page and Sergey Brin control the company despite owning a minority of the total shares.

### 1.4 Conflicts Within Ownership Structures

This is one of the **most testable** concepts in this module. There are two main types of conflicts:

#### [[Principal-Agent Problem]]

**Definition:** A conflict between the **owners** (principals/shareholders) and the **managers** (agents) of a company.

**When does it arise?** Under [[dispersed ownership]] with [[dispersed voting power]].

- Shareholders = **weak** (too fragmented to control anything)
- Managers = **strong** (no one is watching them closely)

> **Analogy:** You hire a property manager to rent out your apartment. You want maximum rent and minimal spending on repairs. The manager wants an easy life — maybe they let their friends rent at below-market rates, or they renovate the kitchen (hiring their cousin's company) when it wasn't needed. That's the [[principal-agent problem]].

**Key insight:** Managers may use company resources to pursue **their own interests** (empire building, excessive perks, job security) rather than maximizing [[shareholder value]].

#### [[Principal-Principal Problem]]

**Definition:** A conflict between the **[[Controlling shareholders|controlling shareholders]]** and the **[[Minority shareholders|minority shareholders]]**.

**When does it arise?** Under [[concentrated ownership]] with [[concentrated voting power]], OR under [[dispersed ownership]] with [[concentrated voting power]] (e.g., through [[dual-class shares]]).

- [[Controlling shareholders|Controlling shareholders]] = **strong** (they run the show)
- [[Minority shareholders|Minority shareholders]] = **weak** (they have no real say)

> **Analogy:** Imagine a group project at school. One kid does all the decision-making and gives themselves the best parts of the project. The other group members contributed money (their grades are on the line) but have no say. The "leader" might even make decisions that benefit them at the expense of the group — that's the [[principal-principal problem]].

**Key insight:** [[Controlling shareholders|Controlling shareholders]] can engage in [[related-party transactions]] — deals that benefit them personally at the expense of [[Minority shareholders|minority shareholders]]. For example, making the company buy supplies from another business the controlling family owns, at inflated prices.

### 1.5 Types of Influential Shareholders

| Shareholder Type | Key Characteristic | Potential Conflict |
|---|---|---|
| **[[Banks]]** | Hold both debt (loans) and equity in companies (common in Europe/Asia) | May push companies to take on large loans on unfavorable terms to benefit the bank's lending business |
| **[[Families]]** | Predominant in Latin America, Asia, Europe | Benefit: reduced [[principal-agent problem]] (owner IS the manager). Risk: poor [[Transparency|transparency]], weak minority shareholder rights, [[interlocking directorates]] |
| **[[State-Owned Enterprises]] (SOEs)** | Government owns part or all of the company | May prioritize social/political goals over [[shareholder value]]; implicit government guarantee reduces market scrutiny |
| **[[Institutional investors]]** | [[Mutual funds|Mutual funds]], [[Pension funds|pension funds]], insurance companies, [[Hedge funds|hedge funds]] | Can promote good governance by holding boards accountable; typically don't have controlling positions in dispersed markets |
| **[[Group Companies]]** | Cross-holding arrangements (e.g., Samsung, Grupo Carso) | Restrict transfer of ownership; risk of [[related-party transactions]] at expense of [[Minority shareholders|minority shareholders]] |
| **[[Private Equity Firms]]** | [[Venture capital|Venture capital]] and LBO firms | Can improve governance by implementing codes and performance-based compensation |
| **[[Foreign Investors]]** | Especially significant in emerging markets | May demand higher [[Transparency|transparency]] and accountability standards |
| **[[Managers and Board Directors]] (Insiders)** | When they are also shareholders | Aligned interests up to a point; large positions may lead to entrenchment |

### 1.6 Effects of Ownership Structure on Corporate Governance

#### [[Director Independence]]

**Definition:** [[Independent directors]] are board members with **no material relationship** with the company (no employment, ownership interest, or remuneration ties).

**Why it matters:** [[Independent|Independent]] directors act as a check on management — they don't have personal incentives to go along with whatever the CEO wants.

- **Dispersed ownership countries** (US, UK): Higher % of [[Independent|independent]] directors needed because the [[principal-agent problem]] is the main concern
- **Concentrated ownership countries**: [[Independent|Independent]] directors serve a narrower role because [[Controlling shareholders|controlling shareholders]] already monitor management
- Most [[OECD]] countries recommend **20%–50%** [[Independent|independent]] directors (or more)

**Real-world example:** After the [[Enron]] scandal (early 2000s), US regulators increased requirements for independent directors on audit, nomination, and compensation committees.

#### [[Board Structures]]

##### [[One-Tier Board]]

**Definition:** A **single board** of directors with both [[executive directors]] (insiders — company management) and [[non-executive directors]] (outsiders).

- Most common structure globally
- Used in the US, UK, and many other countries

##### [[Two-Tier Board]]

**Definition:** Two separate boards — a [[supervisory board]] that oversees a [[management board]].

- The supervisory board monitors, inspects books, reviews annual reports, and sets compensation
- Mandated in **Germany, Argentina, Russia**
- Optional in **Brazil, France**
- In Germany, the supervisory board includes representatives from banks and labor groups

> **Analogy:** A one-tier board is like a classroom where the teacher (management) and the [[Principal|principal]] (oversight) sit in the same room. A two-tier board is like having the [[Principal|principal]] observe from behind a one-way mirror — separate but watching.

#### [[Special Voting Arrangements]]

Some countries protect [[Minority shareholders]] through special voting rules:
- **Brazil, India, UK, Italy, Israel, Turkey, Portugal**: Have arrangements that help minority shareholders participate in board nominations
- In the **UK**, for a "premium listing" when there's a controlling shareholder, independent directors must be separately approved by both the full shareholder base AND non-controlling shareholders

#### [[Corporate Governance Codes]]

- Many countries adopt **"comply or explain"** codes — companies either follow best practices or explain why they don't
- Some use **company law** (Chile) or **stock exchange listing requirements** (India)
- **Japan:** Companies without outside directors must justify why they don't have any

#### [[Stewardship Codes]]

**Definition:** Voluntary codes that encourage **investors** (not companies) to exercise their rights and engage in [[Corporate governance]].

- The **UK Stewardship Code** requires UK asset managers to publish a "comply or explain" statement about how they engage with companies they invest in

## Section 2: Evaluating Corporate Governance Policies and Practices

### 2.1 Why Effective Governance Matters

Effective [[Corporate governance]] acts like a company's immune system — it protects against bad decisions, conflicts of interest, and mismanagement.

| Good Governance Outcomes | Bad Governance Outcomes |
|---|---|
| Higher profitability | Reputational damage |
| Growth in [[return on equity]] | Reduced competitiveness |
| Better access to credit | Share price weakness/[[Volatility|volatility]] |
| Higher, sustainable dividends | Reduced profitability |
| Favorable long-term share performance | Higher [[Cost of capital]] |
| Lower [[Cost of capital]] | — |

**How analysts use governance analysis:** They typically adjust the **[[Risk premium]]** ([[Cost of capital|cost of capital]]) or **[[credit spread]]** of a company to reflect their governance assessment. Poor governance → higher risk → higher [[Discount rate|discount rate]] → lower [[Valuation|valuation]].

### 2.2 Board Policies and Practices

The board of directors is the **first thing investors look at** when evaluating governance. Here's what they examine:

#### [[Board Structure]] — One-Tier vs. Two-Tier
- Does the structure provide sufficient oversight, representation, and accountability?

#### [[CEO Duality]]

**Definition:** When the [[CEO]] also serves as the [[chairperson of the board]].

**Why it's a red flag:** The CEO is supposed to run the company. The chairperson is supposed to oversee the CEO. When the same person does both, it's like being your own referee — the monitoring function is compromised.

**Mitigation:** If there's CEO duality or a non-independent chairperson, the company may appoint a [[lead independent director]] to help protect investor interests.

**Real-world example:** JPMorgan Chase combined the CEO and chairman roles under Jamie Dimon. [[Institutional investors|Institutional investors]] periodically push to separate the roles, arguing it weakens oversight.

#### [[Board Independence]]

- A board **lacking independent directors** is a **negative governance signal**
- Without independent directors, management can act in a self-serving manner
- This **increases investors' perception of risk**

#### [[Board Committees]]

Common committees include:

| Committee | Purpose | Why Independence Matters |
|---|---|---|
| **[[Audit Committee]]** | Oversees financial reporting integrity | Non-independent members → potential manipulation of financials |
| **[[Remuneration Committee]]** (Compensation) | Sets executive pay | Non-independent members → may set excessive pay |
| **[[Nomination Committee]]** | Selects new directors | Non-independent members → may pick friends/allies |
| **[[Risk and Compliance Committee]]** | Monitors [[Risk management|risk management]] | Non-independent members → may ignore inconvenient risks |
| **[[Governance Committee]]** | Oversees governance practices | Ensures accountability |

#### [[Board Skills and Experience]]

- Directors should have expertise **relevant to the company's core operations**
- In industries with large ESG risks (mining, energy), board members should have expertise in environmental, climate, or social issues
- A board where everyone has the same background → **groupthink risk**

#### [[Board Tenure]]

**Definition:** How long a director has served on the board.

- **Long tenure** (>10 years per many governance codes) is a **double-edged sword:**
  - **Positive:** Deep understanding of the business
  - **Negative:** May become too aligned with management, resistant to change, or lose independence

#### [[Board Composition]]

Reflects the **number and diversity** of directors — professional background, cultural background, geography, gender, age, and tenure.

- Boards that are **too large** or **lack diversity** may govern less effectively
- Long-tenured, homogeneous boards risk becoming controlling, self-serving, or resistant to new practices

#### [[Board Evaluation]]

- Can be done internally (**self-evaluation**) or by an outside party (**external review**)
- Covers: performance of duties, leadership, structure, committees, and board-management interaction
- Some boards evaluate "as needed," others do periodic external reviews

### 2.3 Worked Example: Evaluating a Board

> **Style (fictional Italian clothing retailer, founded by the Donato family):**
> - 11-member board, chairperson ≠ CEO ✅ (no [[CEO duality]])
> - 2 independent directors out of 11 ❌ (very low independence)
> - 4 Donato family members on the board, including the chair ❌ (family [[Dominance|dominance]])
> - Family members have served 20+ years ❌ (far exceeds 10-year [[Benchmark|benchmark]])
> - 5 women on the board ✅ (gender diversity)
> - Age range: 35–75 ✅ (age diversity)
>
> **Verdict:** Despite good diversity, the board has **substandard independence** and **excessive family control with long tenures**. This raises [[Corporate governance]] risk.

### 2.4 Executive Remuneration

**Definition:** How executives (especially the CEO) get paid.

Key evaluation points:

| Factor | What to Look For |
|---|---|
| **[[Transparency|Transparency]]** | Is compensation clearly disclosed? |
| **Performance criteria** | Are short-term and long-term incentive plans tied to meaningful [[KPIs]]? |
| **Linkage to strategy** | Does the pay structure incentivize behavior aligned with long-term company value? |
| **CEO-to-worker pay ratio** | Is the ratio excessive? |
| **[[Say-on-Pay]]** | Can shareholders vote on remuneration? |
| **[[Clawback Policy]]** | Can the company recover pay if misconduct or financial restatements occur? |

> **Analogy:** Imagine your school offered teachers a bonus. If the bonus is based on students' test scores, teachers are incentivized to teach well (aligned with the school's goals). If the bonus is based on how many students are enrolled (regardless of learning), the teacher might focus on marketing instead of teaching. Executive remuneration works the same way — the **incentive structure drives behavior**.

### 2.5 Shareholder Voting Rights

#### [[Straight voting]]
**Definition:** One share = one vote. Simple and democratic.

#### [[Dual-Class Shares]]
**Definition:** Founders/management get shares with **more voting power** than public shareholders.

- Creates a potential [[conflict of interest]] between insiders and minority shareholders
- Investors must be aware of this structure when investing

**Real-world example:** Snap Inc. (Snapchat) went [[Public offering|public offering]] shares with **zero voting rights** to the public. Founders retained all control. Many index providers debated whether to even include Snap in their indices.

## Section 3: Identifying ESG-Related Risks and Opportunities

### 3.1 The Core Challenge

The biggest challenge with ESG analysis: **getting data that is relevant, comparable, and decision-useful**.

**Why is this hard?**
- ESG disclosures are mostly **voluntary** (unlike financial statements, which are required)
- Companies report ESG metrics **inconsistently** — Company A might report carbon emissions per dollar of revenue, Company B might report total tons, Company C might not report at all
- Some companies emphasize positive ESG info and hide the negative stuff

### 3.2 Materiality and Investment Horizon

#### [[Materiality]] (ESG Context)

**Definition:** ESG-related issues that are **expected to affect a company's operations, financial performance, and the [[Valuation|valuation]] of its securities**.

> **Analogy:** If you're evaluating a restaurant, the fact that they use a lot of water is *material*. The fact that the owner drives a gas-guzzling car to work is not — it doesn't affect the restaurant's operations.

**Key nuance:** What's material depends on the **industry**:

| Industry | Most Material ESG Factor | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Energy / Steel | **Environmental** | Heavy resource use, emissions, [[Regulatory risk|regulatory risk]] |
| Banking | **Social** (data privacy, customer satisfaction) | Handles personal financial data, public trust is essential |
| Pharmaceuticals | **Social** (product safety, quality) | Drug recalls and safety scandals can be devastating |
| All industries | **Governance** | Board structure, executive pay, and accountability affect every company |

#### [[Investment Horizon]]

**Why it matters:** The same ESG issue can be material or immaterial depending on your time frame.

> **Example:** A steel company operating in a developing country with weak environmental regulations. A **short-term investor** sees no immediate problem — regulations are lax, and profits are fine. A **long-term investor** sees a ticking time bomb — stricter regulations are coming, and costly plant upgrades or fines will crush profitability.

**Important:** ESG factors can **jump from long-term to short-term** suddenly — an oil spill, a data breach, or a regulatory change can make a "long-term" ESG issue an immediate crisis.

### 3.3 Three Approaches to Identifying ESG Factors

#### Approach 1: [[Proprietary Methods]]

**What it is:** Analysts use **their own judgment** and **their firm's tools** to research ESG factors.

**Sources:** Annual reports, sustainability reports, proxy reports, regulatory filings (10-K), company websites, news reports, industry associations, environmental groups, government organizations.

**Problem:** Data is inconsistent across companies because disclosure is voluntary. What Company A reports might not be comparable to what Company B reports.

#### Approach 2: [[ESG Data Providers]]

**What it is:** Third-party vendors like **MSCI** or **Sustainalytics** provide ESG scores and rankings for companies.

**Problem:** These scores involve **subjective interpretation**. Different providers can give the same company very different ESG scores based on their own methodologies.

#### Approach 3: [[Not-for-Profit Industry Initiatives]]

**What it is:** Organizations that develop standardized frameworks for ESG disclosure.

**Examples:**
- **[[GRI]] (Global Reporting Initiative):** Develops sustainability reporting standards with specific topics by industry
- **[[SASB]] (Sustainability Accounting Standards Board):** Creates materiality maps showing which ESG factors matter most for each industry
- **[[Access to Nutrition Index]]:** Evaluates food companies' policies related to obesity and undernutrition

**Benefit:** These promote **standardized, comparable** ESG disclosures — but adoption is voluntary.

### 3.4 Green Bonds

**Definition:** [[Green bonds]] are bonds where the **proceeds are earmarked for environmental or climate-related projects**.

Key characteristics:
- Typically have the **same credit rating** as the issuer's conventional bonds
- Same bondholder recourse as conventional bonds
- Analysis and [[Valuation|valuation]] are **essentially the same** as conventional bonds
- May trade at a **premium** (tighter [[credit spread]]) due to market demand

**Unique risk — [[Greenwashing]]:** The risk that the bond proceeds are **not actually used** for beneficial environmental projects. This means the investor overpaid for a "green" label that doesn't deliver real impact.

**[[Liquidity|Liquidity]]ty risk|[[Liquidity|Liquidity]] risk]]:** Green bonds are often bought by buy-and-hold investors, so they may be harder to sell.

## Section 4: Evaluating ESG-Related Risks and Opportunities

### 4.1 ESG Integration: The Framework

[[ESG integration]] means incorporating qualitative and quantitative ESG factors into **traditional security analysis** and **portfolio construction**.

#### How ESG Integration Differs for Equity vs. Fixed Income

This is a **critical distinction** for the exam:

| Dimension | [[Equity Analysis]] | [[Fixed-Income Analysis]] |
|---|---|---|
| **Goal** | Identify opportunities **AND** mitigate downside risk | Primarily **mitigate downside risk** (bond redeems at par — no upside beyond coupon) |
| **[[Valuation|Valuation]] adjustment** | Adjust [[Discount rate]], [[valuation multiples]], [[Terminal value]] | Adjust [[credit spread]] or [[CDS]] |
| **Financial adjustments** | Revenue forecasts, operating costs, margins, earnings, capex | Credit ratios, cash flow, [[Liquidity|liquidity]], internal credit assessments |
| **Analysis tools** | Sensitivity/[[Scenario analysis|scenario analysis]], forecasted financials | Relative value analysis, spread analysis, duration analysis |

> **Why the difference?** A stock has **unlimited upside** — if a company improves its ESG profile, the stock can go up a lot. A bond has **capped upside** — even if the company becomes the most sustainable business on earth, you still just get your coupon and [[Par value|par value]] back. So for bonds, the focus is on making sure ESG risks don't cause the company to **default** or see its credit spread **widen**.

### 4.2 ESG Integration Steps

```
Step 1: Identify material ESG factors for the company/industry
         ↓
Step 2: Evaluate historical and forecast ESG data (vs. peers)
         ↓
Step 3: Adjust financial statements
        • Income statement: revenues, operating costs, margins, earnings
        • Cash flow statement: operating cash flow, capex
        • Balance sheet: impaired assets
         ↓
Step 4: Adjust valuation
        • Equity: discount rate, multiples, terminal value
        • Bonds: credit spread, CDS
```

### 4.3 Example: Environmental Factors — Frizzle Drinks (Beverage Company)

**Scenario:** [[Frizzle Drinks]] is a large US-based non-alcoholic beverage company operating in developed and emerging markets, including water-scarce countries. Water is a key ingredient — so [[water intensity]] (liters of water used per liter of beverage produced) is a critical ESG metric.

**The analysis:**

**Step 1 — Identify the material factor:** Water intensity (environmental)

**Step 2 — Evaluate the data:**
- Frizzle reduced water intensity by **27%** from 2009–2021
- Targeting an additional **13%** reduction by 2025
- Frizzle's water intensity has decreased **more than peers** in recent years

**Step 3 — Adjust financial statements:**
- Before ESG adjustment: COGS/Revenue = **40%** (peers: 42%)
- ESG adjustment: Frizzle's water efficiency reduces COGS/Revenue by an additional **1%** → COGS/Revenue = **39%**
- Result: Gross margin **improves** relative to peers

**Step 4 — Adjust valuation:**
- **Equity:** Lower COGS → higher forecast earnings → higher fair value for the stock
- **Bonds/CDS:** Improved operating cash flow → analyst checks whether this lower ESG risk is already reflected in current spreads and adjusts accordingly

### 4.4 Example: Social Factors — Well Pharma (Pharmaceutical Company)

**Scenario:** [[Well Pharma]] is a European pharmaceutical company with the **worst track record** among peers for product recalls and regulatory warning letters. Four major drug quality and safety scandals in five years.

**The material social factors:**

| Factor | Description |
|---|---|
| Product quality controversies | Damage to brand → decreased sales |
| Regulatory warning letters | Increased compliance costs |
| Product recalls | Lost revenue + recall costs |
| Regulatory fines | Provisions for litigation and returns |
| Product quality certifications % | Lower % → higher risk of future quality issues |

**Financial statement adjustments:**
- **Revenue:** Decreased by **2%** due to existing product quality controversies
- **COGS/Revenue:** Increased by **1.3%** to reflect additional manufacturing investments needed
- **Non-operating expenses:** Additional **4.5%** of operating income for restructuring charges and non-recurring costs

**Valuation impact:** Significant negative impact on both stock valuation (lower earnings) and bond valuation (weaker cash flow). Brand impairment is an additional risk.

### 4.5 Example: Governance Factors — Sumiyoshi Banking Group (Japanese Bank)

**Scenario:** [[Sumiyoshi]] is a Japanese bank holding company. The analyst compares its governance profile to its domestic peer group.

**Governance comparison (Sumiyoshi vs. Peers):**

| Factor | Peer Group | Sumiyoshi | Assessment |
|---|---|---|---|
| Board type | Two-tier | Two-tier | Neutral |
| CEO duality | Yes | Yes | Negative (both have this issue) |
| Independent chairperson | Yes | **No** | ❌ Negative |
| Board independence | 47% | **36%** | ❌ Below peers |
| Gender diversity | 17% female | **7% female** | ❌ Well below peers |
| Long tenure (>10 years) | 0% | **14%** | ❌ Some directors entrenched |
| Industry experience | 67% | **20%** | ❌ Lack of relevant expertise |
| Say-on-pay | Yes | **No** | ❌ Shareholders can't vote on pay |
| Number of committees | 5 | **4** | Missing governance committee |

**Credit risk proxy:** The analyst uses [[non-performing loans]] (NPLs) as a proxy for governance risk in banking. Sumiyoshi's NPL ratio is **50 basis points higher** than its peer group average.

**Valuation impact:**
- **Equity:** Increase the [[Risk premium]] embedded in the stock valuation
- **Bonds:** Increase the [[credit spread]] relative to peers

### 4.6 Putting It All Together: The Practice Problem Framework

When the exam gives you a company and asks you to evaluate ESG factors, follow this checklist:

```
1. OWNERSHIP STRUCTURE
   □ Dispersed or concentrated?
   □ What type of conflict exists? (principal-agent or principal-principal)
   □ Any dual-class shares or pyramid structures?

2. GOVERNANCE EVALUATION
   □ CEO duality?
   □ Board independence %?
   □ Board diversity (gender, age, background)?
   □ Board tenure (any >10 years)?
   □ Relevant board committees in place?
   □ Say-on-pay and clawback policies?
   □ Straight or dual-class voting?

3. ESG FACTOR IDENTIFICATION
   □ Which E, S, or G factor is most material for this industry?
   □ What is the investment horizon?
   □ What data source is being used? (proprietary, ESG vendor, not-for-profit)

4. ESG INTEGRATION
   □ Equity or fixed income?
   □ What financial statement adjustments are needed?
   □ What valuation adjustment is appropriate?
      • Equity → discount rate, multiples
      • Fixed income → credit spread, CDS
```

## Key Exam Takeaways

1. **[[Dispersed ownership]]** → [[principal-agent problem]] (shareholders vs. managers)
2. **[[Concentrated ownership]]** → [[principal-principal problem]] (controlling vs. minority shareholders)
3. **[[Dual-class shares]]** can give control to founders with minority economic ownership
4. **[[CEO duality]]** = CEO + Board Chair is the same person → governance red flag
5. **[[Independent directors]]**: OECD recommends 20%–50%+; more important in dispersed ownership
6. **[[Materiality]]** depends on **industry** (E for energy, S for pharma, G for everyone)
7. **[[Investment horizon]]** affects which ESG factors matter — long-term issues can become short-term overnight
8. **Equity [[ESG integration|ESG integration]]:** Opportunities + downside risk → adjust [[Discount rate]]
9. **Fixed-income [[ESG integration|ESG integration]]:** Downside risk only → adjust [[credit spread]]/[[CDS]]
10. **Three ESG data sources:** Proprietary research, ESG data providers, not-for-profit initiatives
11. **[[Green bonds]]** are valued like conventional bonds but face [[greenwashing]] risk
12. **[[Clawback policies]]** and **[[say-on-pay]]** provisions are signs of good governance
