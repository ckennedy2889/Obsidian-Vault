# Module 1: Analysis of Dividends and Share Repurchases

---

## Overview

This module covers how companies return cash to [[shareholders]] — through [[dividends]] or [[share repurchases]] — and why these decisions matter. Think of it like this: a company earns profits, and then the board has to decide: *"Do we keep this money and reinvest it, or give some back to the people who own us?"*

That decision — the [[Payout policy]] — affects stock prices, financial ratios, tax bills, and how investors perceive the company.

---

## 1. Dividends: Forms and Effects on Shareholder Wealth

### What Is a Dividend?

A [[Dividend]] is a distribution paid to [[shareholders]], authorized by a company's [[board of directors]]. Unlike [[interest payments]] on [[bonds]] (which are legally required), dividends are **discretionary** — the company *chooses* to pay them.

> **Real-World Analogy:** Imagine you and your friends own a lemonade stand. At the end of the summer, you have $500 in profit. You could reinvest that money into a better stand for next year, or you could split the cash among yourselves. That split is essentially a dividend.

### Key Dates

- **[[Declaration date]]:** The board announces the dividend.
- **[[Ex-dividend date]]:** The first day the stock trades *without* the right to receive the declared dividend. On this date, the stock price typically drops by roughly the dividend amount.
- **[[Record date]]:** The company checks who owns shares and is entitled to the dividend.
- **[[Payment date]]:** Cash hits your account.

> **Why does the price drop on the [[Ex-dividend|ex-dividend]]nd date|[[Ex-dividend|ex-dividend]] date]]?** Because the dividend is no longer "attached" to the stock. If a stock is worth $50 and pays a $2 dividend, the day after the ex-date it should trade near $48 — you've already extracted $2 of value.

---

### Types of Dividends

#### [[Regular Cash Dividends]]

These are paid on a predictable schedule. The frequency varies by market:

| Market | Most Common Frequency |
|---|---|
| **US, Canada** | Quarterly |
| **Australia, Japan, Saudi Arabia** | Semiannually |
| **Egypt, Germany, Thailand** | Annually |

**Why do companies pay regular dividends?** A [[Consistent|consistent]] track record signals [[profitability]] and management confidence. Investors interpret rising dividends as a sign the company is healthy and growing.

**Real-World Example:** Companies like Procter & Gamble or Johnson & Johnson have increased dividends for 50+ consecutive years. They're called "[[Dividend Aristocrats]]."

#### [[Extra (Special/Irregular) Dividends]]

A one-time bonus payment, often triggered by a windfall event.

**Real-World Example:** In 2018, Tencent Holdings declared a [[Special dividend|special dividend]] of HKD 250 million after its [[Spin-off|spin-off]] Tencent Music went public. This was a one-time event — don't expect it next quarter.

**Why use special dividends?** Companies in [[cyclical industries]] (like mining or oil) may have great years and terrible years. Rather than committing to a high regular dividend they might have to cut later, they pay a small regular dividend plus a [[Special dividend|special dividend]] in boom years.

#### [[Liquidating Dividends]]

A [[Liquidating dividend]] is a **return of capital**, not a distribution of earnings. It happens when:

- A company is shutting down and distributing remaining assets
- A company sells off a division and passes the cash to shareholders
- A dividend exceeds [[accumulated retained earnings]] (eating into the original [[invested capital]])

> **Why does this distinction matter?** Because a [[Liquidating dividend|liquidating dividend]] is giving you back *your own money* (capital), not profits. It's like returning the [[Principal|principal]] on a loan vs. paying interest.

#### [[Stock Dividends]] (Bonus Issues)

Instead of cash, the company gives you more shares. Typically 2–10% of existing shares.

**Key Insight: Stock dividends do NOT make you wealthier.** Here's why:

| | Before 3% [[Stock dividend|Stock Dividend]] | After 3% [[Stock dividend|Stock Dividend]] |
|---|---|---|
| Shares Outstanding | 1,000,000 | 1,030,000 |
| [[EPS]] | $1.00 | $0.97 |
| Stock Price | $20.00 | $19.42 |
| [[P/E Ratio]] | 20x | 20x |
| Total [[Market value|Market Value]] | $20,000,000 | $20,000,000 |
| Your Shares (10% owner) | 100,000 | 103,000 |
| **Your Wealth** | **$2,000,000** | **$2,000,000** |

> **Analogy:** Imagine you have a pizza cut into 8 slices. A [[Stock dividend|stock dividend]] is like cutting it into 10 slices instead. You have more slices, but the same amount of pizza.

**Effects on [[Financial Ratios]]:**

- **[[Cash dividends]]** → Reduce [[Assets]] (cash goes out) and [[shareholders' equity]] (retained earnings decrease) → [[Liquidity ratios]] (like [[Current ratio]]) **decrease**, [[leverage ratios]] (like [[debt-to-equity]]) **increase**
- **[[Stock dividends]]** → No effect on total [[Assets]] or total [[shareholders' equity]]. Retained earnings decrease, but [[contributed capital]] increases by the same amount. [[Liquidity|Liquidity]] and leverage ratios are **unchanged**.

#### [[Stock Splits]]

Similar to stock dividends but larger in scale. A 2-for-1 split doubles your shares and halves the price.

| | Before 2-for-1 Split | After 2-for-1 Split |
|---|---|---|
| Shares Outstanding | 4 million | 8 million |
| Stock Price | €40.00 | €20.00 |
| [[EPS]] | €1.50 | €0.75 |
| [[DPS]] | €0.50 | €0.25 |
| [[Dividend payout ratio]] | 1/3 | 1/3 |
| [[Dividend yield]] | 1.25% | 1.25% |
| [[P/E]] | 26.7x | 26.7x |
| [[Market value|Market Value]] of Equity | €160 million | €160 million |

**Why split?** Companies believe there's an "optimal price range" (historically $20–$80 in the US) that attracts more investors. A $500 stock might scare off small investors — a $50 stock feels more accessible.

**Accounting difference:** A [[Stock dividend]] transfers value from [[retained earnings]] to [[contributed capital]]. A [[Stock split]] does not affect any equity accounts at all — it just changes the share count and [[Par value]].

#### [[Reverse Stock Splits]]

The opposite: fewer shares, higher price.

**Real-World Example:** Globus Maritime was warned by NASDAQ that its share price fell below the $1 minimum listing requirement. It did a 1-for-10 reverse split — every 10 shares became 1 share. If the price was $4.25 before, the new price would be $42.50.

**Why do this?** To avoid getting [[delisted]] from an exchange, or to attract [[Institutional investors]] who often won't buy stocks below $5.

> **Key principle:** Neither stock splits nor reverse splits change [[shareholder wealth]]. They're cosmetic changes.

## 2. Dividend Policy Theories

The big question: **Does it matter how much a company pays in dividends?**

Three major theories offer different answers:

### Theory 1: [[Dividend Irrelevance]] (Miller & Modigliani)

**The claim:** In a perfect world (no taxes, no transaction costs, symmetric information), [[Dividend policy|dividend policy]] doesn't matter.

**The logic:**

Suppose a company earns $10,000 and has 10,000 shares at $20 each. It can:

- **Pay a $1 dividend:** Price drops to $19. Shareholder has $19 stock + $1 cash = $20.
- **Pay no dividend:** Price stays at $20. Shareholder still has $20.

Either way, wealth = $20. The shareholder can always create a "[[homemade dividend]]" by selling some shares if they need cash.

**Why this matters even though it's unrealistic:** It's a [[Benchmark|benchmark]]. The theory tells us that if dividends *do* matter, it must be because of [[market imperfections]] like taxes, [[transaction costs]], or [[information asymmetry]].

**Real-World Complications:**

- [[Flotation costs]] (fees for issuing new shares) can be 4–10% of capital raised
- Selling shares to create homemade dividends incurs [[transaction costs]] and possibly [[capital gains taxes]]
- If share prices are volatile, you might have to sell more shares than expected to generate income

### Theory 2: [[Bird in the Hand]] Argument

**The claim:** Investors prefer dividends now because they're less risky than uncertain future [[capital gains]].

> **Analogy:** Would you rather have $100 cash today, or a promise that your investment might grow by $100 over the next year? Most people take the cash. That's the "bird in the hand."

**Implication:** Companies that pay dividends should have a **lower [[Cost of equity]]** (investors demand less return because they're getting certainty) and thus a **higher share price**.

**MM's rebuttal:** Paying a dividend today just reduces the [[Ex-dividend|ex-dividend]] price by the same amount. The risk of future cash flows hasn't changed — you've just rearranged the timing.

### Theory 3: [[Tax Preference]] Theory

**The claim:** In countries where [[dividends]] are taxed more heavily than [[capital gains]], investors should prefer companies that reinvest earnings rather than pay dividends.

**Why?**

1. Even if tax *rates* are equal, capital gains taxes can be **deferred** until you sell the stock. Deferral = free loan from the government.
2. In some countries (like the US and Australia), shares held at death receive a "[[step-up in basis]]," meaning the capital gains tax is never paid.
3. In mainland China, dividends on shares held > 1 year are taxed at 0%, but shares held < 1 month are taxed at 20%.

**Extreme version:** This theory would advocate a **zero** [[Dividend payout ratio]] — return cash via [[share repurchases]] instead (taxed as capital gains).

### Summary Comparison

| Theory | Dividend Policy Effect | Preferred Payout |
|---|---|---|
| [[MM Irrelevance]] | No effect on value | Doesn't matter |
| [[Bird in Hand]] | More dividends → higher value | High dividends |
| [[Tax Preference]] | More dividends → lower value (if dividends taxed higher) | Low/no dividends, share repurchases |

## 3. Signaling Theory: What Dividends Tell Us

### [[Information Asymmetry]] and [[Dividend Signaling]]

Managers know more about a company's future than outside investors. Dividends can be a **credible signal** because they involve real cash.

**Why is this signal hard to fake?** A company with weak future [[cash flows]] can't sustain a dividend increase for long. It would eventually have to cut the dividend or borrow — both bad for the stock price. So only companies genuinely confident in their future would increase dividends.

### What Different Dividend Actions Signal

| Dividend Action | Typical Signal | Typical Price Effect |
|---|---|---|
| **Initiation** | Management confident in sustainable earnings | Positive |
| **Increase** | Future earnings expected to grow | Positive |
| **Decrease/Cut** | Earnings problems or need for financial flexibility | Usually Negative |
| **Omission** | Serious financial stress | Strongly Negative |

### Real-World Examples

**Positive signal from initiation:**
- **Oracle (2009):** Initiated a $0.05/quarter dividend during the financial crisis. Analysts saw it as a signal Oracle was strong enough to weather the downturn. The dividend was tiny relative to $8 billion in operating cash flow — it was all about the *message*.

**Paradoxical positive signal from a cut:**
- **BT Group (2018):** Cut its interim dividend from 4.85p to 4.62p per share. Despite a 71% drop in operating cash flow, investors saw the cut as "prudent" — the stock rose 6.9% the day of the announcement. The market viewed it as responsible management rather than panic.

**Dividend initiation as maturity signal:**
- **eBay (2019):** Initiated its first-ever dividend 24 years after founding. Some saw it as broadening the investor base; others saw it as an admission that growth was slowing. Legacy tech companies (Apple 2012, Cisco 2011, Microsoft 2003) followed the same pattern.

### Characteristics of Companies That Consistently Increase Dividends

- Dominant or niche positions in their industry
- Global operations
- Relatively stable (less volatile) earnings
- High [[return on assets]]
- Low [[debt ratios]]

## 4. Agency Costs and Dividends

### The [[Overinvestment Problem]] ([[Jensen's Free Cash Flow Hypothesis]])

[[Agency costs]] arise because managers (agents) may not always act in [[shareholders]]' (owners') best interests. One key concern: managers might invest in **negative [[NPV]] projects** that grow the company's size (and the manager's power/compensation) but destroy shareholder value.

> **Analogy:** Imagine you hire someone to manage your money. Instead of returning your profits, they keep investing in increasingly risky ventures because their bonus is tied to how much money they manage, not how well they perform. That's the overinvestment problem.

**Solution: Pay dividends.** If the company pays out all [[Free cash flow to equity]] as dividends, managers have no extra cash to waste on bad projects.

**When is this most concerning?**

- Large, mature companies with few growth opportunities but lots of cash flow
- Companies with weak [[Corporate governance]] (e.g., the CEO is also the board chair)

**When is holding cash reasonable?**

- Growing companies in fast-changing industries (need flexibility)
- Companies facing potential credit crunches (Ford accumulated cash in the 1990s, which helped it survive 2008)

### The [[Shareholder-Bondholder Conflict]]

Paying dividends can also **hurt** bondholders:

- Dividends reduce the cash cushion available for fixed interest payments
- Large dividends effectively transfer wealth from [[bondholders]] to [[shareholders]]
- This is why [[bond indentures]] often include [[Covenants]] restricting dividend payments (e.g., minimum [[interest coverage ratios]], maximum [[debt-to-equity ratios]])

### The Utility Company Paradox

Electric utilities pay high dividends and then issue new equity — which seems wasteful given [[flotation costs]]. But there's a clever agency logic:

1. Utilities are monopolies regulated by politically motivated regulators
2. Regulators might set rates too low (to please voters)
3. By paying high dividends and then going to the [[Capital markets|capital markets]], the utility forces market discipline
4. If regulators set rates too low → stock price falls → utility can't raise capital → customers lose power → regulators face angry voters

The dividend-and-reissue cycle acts as a **monitoring mechanism** for both managers and regulators.

## 5. Factors Affecting Dividend Policy in Practice

Six real-world factors influence dividend decisions:

### 5.1 [[Investment Opportunities]]

**Rule:** More profitable investment opportunities → lower [[Dividend payout ratio]]

**Why?** Internally generated cash is cheaper than issuing new equity (no [[flotation costs]]). If the company has great projects to fund, it should keep the cash.

**Example:** [[Technology|Technology]] companies (rapid pace of change, capital-intensive R&D) have much lower [[dividend yields]] than utilities (slow-changing, fewer investment opportunities).

### 5.2 Expected Volatility of [[Future Earnings]]

**Rule:** More volatile earnings → more cautious about raising dividends

**Why?** Managers are extremely reluctant to **cut** dividends. If you raise the dividend during a boom and then earnings crash, you're stuck — cutting the dividend sends a terrible signal. So companies with volatile earnings keep dividends conservative and "smooth" them over time.

### 5.3 [[Financial flexibility]]

**Rule:** Need for [[Financial flexibility|financial flexibility]] → lower dividends or use share repurchases instead

**Real-World Example:** In 2019, Skanska AB (Swedish construction giant) cut its dividend 30% to preserve SKr920 million annually. Despite an initial 9% share price drop, the stock recovered within two months — the market respected the precautionary move.

**Why repurchases help with flexibility:** Unlike dividends, a [[Share repurchase|share repurchase]] program creates **no obligation** to continue. You can announce a $500 million buyback and then quietly not follow through if cash gets tight.

### 5.4 [[Tax Considerations]]

Three main tax systems determine how dividends are taxed:

#### [[Double taxation system]]

Corporate profits are taxed **twice** — once at the corporate level, then again when distributed as dividends.

**Worked Example:**

$$\text{Effective Double Tax Rate} = \frac{\text{Corporate Tax} + \text{Personal Tax on Dividend}}{\text{Pretax Earnings}}$$

| Step | Amount |
|---|---|
| Pretax earnings | $100 |
| Corporate tax (35%) | −$35 |
| After-tax income | $65 |
| Dividend (100% payout) | $65 |
| Personal tax on dividend (15%) | −$9.75 |
| **Net dividend to shareholder** | **$55.25** |
| **Effective tax rate** | **(35 + 9.75) / 100 = 44.75%** |

> Almost 45 cents of every dollar earned is taken by taxes before it reaches the shareholder.

#### [[Dividend Imputation System]] (e.g., Australia, New Zealand)

The goal is to tax corporate earnings only **once** — at the shareholder's marginal rate. Shareholders receive a "[[Franking credit]]" for taxes the [[Corporation|corporation]] already paid.

**Worked Example (Australia):**

| | Shareholder at 15% Rate | Shareholder at 47% Rate |
|---|---|---|
| Pretax income | A$100 | A$100 |
| Corporate tax (30%) | −A$30 | −A$30 |
| Dividend (100% payout) | A$70 | A$70 |
| Tax owed on pretax income | A$15 | A$47 |
| Less: [[Franking credit|franking credit]] | −A$30 | −A$30 |
| **Tax due/(refund)** | **(A$15) refund** | **A$17 owed** |
| **Effective rate** | **15%** | **47%** |

> The low-income shareholder actually gets a **refund** because the company paid more tax than the shareholder's personal rate.

#### [[Split-rate tax system]]

Earnings distributed as dividends are taxed at a **lower corporate rate** than retained earnings. Dividends are then taxed again as [[Personal income|personal income]].

**Worked Example:**

| Step | Amount |
|---|---|
| Pretax earnings allocated to dividends | €100 |
| Corporate tax on distributed earnings (20%) | −€20 |
| Dividends distributed | €80 |
| Personal tax (35%) on €80 | −€28 |
| **Net dividend** | **€52** |
| **Effective rate** | **(20 + 28) / 100 = 48%** |

### 5.5 [[Flotation Costs]]

Issuing new shares is expensive — investment banker fees, legal costs, and potential negative price impact. These costs are proportionally higher for smaller companies. Companies avoid setting dividends so high that they'd need to issue new equity to fund good projects.

### 5.6 Contractual and Legal Restrictions

- **[[Bond covenants]]** may restrict dividends (e.g., must maintain minimum [[Interest coverage]])
- **[[Impairment of capital rules]]** (US, Canada) require maintaining minimum [[Book value]]
- Some countries (e.g., Brazil) **legally mandate** minimum dividend distributions
- **[[Preferred dividends]]** must be paid before common dividends; if preferred dividends are [[cumulative]], arrears must be cleared first

## 6. Payout Policies

### [[Stable dividend policy]]

**What it is:** Dividends are based on **long-term sustainable earnings**, not short-term fluctuations. Dividends grow gradually, even if earnings are volatile.

**Why it's popular:** Managers hate cutting dividends. A stable policy smooths things out — you raise dividends slowly during booms so you don't have to cut them during busts.

#### [[Target Payout Adjustment Model]] (Lintner Model)

This formula shows how companies **gradually adjust** their dividend toward a target:

$$\text{Expected Dividend} = D_{\text{prev}} + \left[\left(E_1 \times \text{Target Payout Ratio} - D_{\text{prev}}\right) \times \text{Adjustment Factor}\right]$$

**What each variable means and WHY it's included:**

| Variable | What It Is | Why It's There |
|---|---|---|
| $D_{\text{prev}}$ | Last year's dividend per share | The starting point — companies build on what they already pay |
| $E_1$ | Expected earnings next year | Forward-looking: the dividend should reflect where earnings are *going* |
| [[Target payout ratio|Target Payout Ratio]]io|Payout Ratio]] | The % of earnings the company *wants* to pay as dividends long-term | The destination — where the company is aiming |
| Adjustment Factor | $\frac{1}{\text{Number of years to adjust}}$ | Controls the speed — a slower adjustment (smaller factor) means less [[Volatility|volatility]] in dividends |

**In plain English:** *"Take the gap between where we want to be (target payout × expected earnings) and where we are now (previous dividend), then close a fraction of that gap this year."*

#### Worked Example

> **Given:**
> - Current dividend: $0.40
> - Expected earnings: $1.50 (up from $1.00 last year)
> - [[Target payout ratio|Target payout ratio]]io|payout ratio]]: 50%
> - Adjustment period: 5 years (adjustment factor = 1/5 = 0.2)

**Step 1:** Calculate the target dividend level

$$E_1 \times \text{Target Payout} = \$1.50 \times 0.50 = \$0.75$$

This is where the company *wants* to be eventually.

**Step 2:** Calculate the gap between target and current

$$\$0.75 - \$0.40 = \$0.35$$

**Step 3:** Close a fraction of that gap

$$\$0.35 \times 0.20 = \$0.07$$

**Step 4:** New dividend

$$\$0.40 + \$0.07 = \$0.47$$

> Even though earnings jumped 50%, the dividend only rose ~17.5%. That's the smoothing effect in action.

**What if earnings then DROP to $1.34?**

$$\text{Expected increase} = (\$1.34 \times 0.50 - \$0.47) \times 0.20 = (\$0.67 - \$0.47) \times 0.20 = \$0.04$$

New dividend = $0.51. The company **still increases** the dividend because $0.51 is still below the target ($0.67). This is exactly what "smoothing" means — short-term earnings drops don't derail the long-term trajectory.

### [[Constant dividend payout ratio policy]]

**What it is:** Pay a fixed percentage of current earnings as dividends every period.

**Formula:**

$$\text{Dividend} = \text{EPS} \times \text{Payout Ratio}$$

**Problem:** Dividends bounce around with earnings. If EPS is $10 one quarter and $4 the next (with a 25% [[Payout ratio|payout ratio]]), dividends go from $2.50 to $1.00. Investors hate that unpredictability.

**When it's used:** Companies with highly [[cyclical earnings]] (like fertilizer producers) may adopt this policy because they can't credibly commit to stable dividends.

**Real-World Example:** Pampas Fertilizer (hypothetical) switched from a stable $1.50/quarter dividend to a 25% constant [[Payout policy|payout policy]] when earnings became too volatile to sustain fixed payments.

### Comparison

| Feature | [[Stable dividend policy]] | [[Constant Payout Ratio Policy]] |
|---|---|---|
| Dividend [[Volatility|volatility]] | Low | High |
| Relationship to earnings | Lagged, smoothed | Direct, immediate |
| Investor certainty | High | Low |
| How common | Very common | Rare |
| Best for | Mature, stable companies | [[Cyclical companies|Cyclical companies]] |

## 7. Global Trends in Payout Policy

Key trends to know:

1. **Fewer companies pay dividends** in most developed markets (US, Canada, EU, UK, Japan) — the fraction has been declining for decades. **Exception:** Asia-Pacific, where the total value of dividends tripled from 2009 to 2019.

2. **Share repurchases are rising** — trending upward in the US since the 1980s, UK/Europe since the 1990s, and now growing rapidly in China and Japan.

3. **Aggregate dividends are increasing** even though fewer companies pay them — dividends are concentrated in a smaller number of large, profitable firms.

4. **Better [[Corporate governance|corporate governance]] reduces dividends** — when information asymmetry decreases (better disclosures, stronger investor protection), the signaling value of dividends declines, so companies feel less pressure to pay them.

## 8. Share Repurchases

### What Is a [[Share repurchase]]?

A [[Share repurchase]] (or [[Buyback]]) is when a company buys back its own shares from the market. The bought-back shares become either [[Treasury stock]] (can be reissued) or [[cancelled shares]] (permanently retired). Either way, they no longer count for [[dividends]], [[voting]], or [[EPS]] calculations.

### Four Repurchase Methods

#### 1. [[Open Market Repurchase]] (Most Common)

The company buys shares on the open market, just like any investor would. 

**Advantages:** Maximum flexibility — no legal obligation to complete the program. Can time purchases to buy when the stock seems undervalued.

**In the US:** No shareholder approval needed. **In Europe:** Shareholder approval required.

#### 2. [[Fixed price tender offer]]

The company offers to buy a specific number of shares at a **fixed price**, usually at a [[Premium]] to the market price. If more shares are tendered than requested, purchases are [[pro rata]].

**Example:** Stock trades at A$37. Company offers to buy 5 million shares at A$40. Fast but expensive.

#### 3. [[Dutch Auction]]

The company specifies a **price range** and asks shareholders to submit the minimum price at which they'd sell. The company fills orders starting from the lowest price until it reaches the desired number of shares, then pays **everyone that price**.

**Example:** Stock at A$37. Company wants 5 million shares, offers A$38–A$40 range. If the clearing price is A$39, everyone who bid A$38–A$39 gets A$39/share.

**Advantage over fixed price tender:** Usually cheaper — the Dutch auction finds the minimum price needed.

#### 4. [[Direct Negotiation]]

The company privately buys shares from a specific large shareholder, often at a premium. Sometimes used defensively:

- **[[Greenmail]]:** Buying out a hostile acquirer at a premium — usually hurts remaining shareholders

**Example:** AfriCitrus has 10 million shares at ZAR 200. An activist buys a large block. The board considers buying back ZAR 500 million worth at ZAR 250/share (ZAR 50 premium). This would only buy 2 million shares, and the post-[[Repurchase price|repurchase price]] would drop to ZAR 187.50 — destroying ZAR 12.50 per share for remaining shareholders.

### Comparison of Methods

| Method | Speed | Cost | Flexibility |
|---|---|---|---|
| Open Market | Slow | Low (can time purchases) | Highest |
| Fixed Price Tender | Fast | High (premium required) | Low |
| Dutch Auction | Moderate | Moderate | Low |
| Direct Negotiation | Fast | Variable (premium or discount) | Case-by-case |

## 9. Financial Statement Effects of Repurchases

### Effect on [[EPS]]

#### Case 1: Repurchase Funded with Surplus Cash

If the cash wasn't earning much return, using it to buy back shares increases EPS because the denominator (share count) shrinks while the numerator ([[Net income|net income]]) stays roughly the same.

**Worked Example:**

> **Takemiya Industries**
> - Shares: 10 million
> - [[Net income|Net income]]: ¥100 million → EPS = ¥10.00
> - Surplus cash: ¥240 million (earning ~0%)
> - [[Repurchase price|Repurchase price]]: ¥140/share

**Step 1:** How many shares can be bought?

$$\frac{¥240,000,000}{¥140} \approx 1,714,286 \text{ shares} \approx 1.7 \text{ million}$$

**Step 2:** Remaining shares

$$10{,}000{,}000 - 1{,}714{,}286 = 8{,}285{,}714 \approx 8.3 \text{ million}$$

**Step 3:** New EPS

$$\text{EPS} = \frac{¥100{,}000{,}000}{8{,}300{,}000} \approx ¥12.05$$

> **EPS increased ~20%** because the cash wasn't earning anything meaningful. You removed dead weight from the [[Balance sheet|balance sheet]].

#### Case 2: Repurchase Funded with Debt

Now the company borrows money to buy back shares. Whether EPS goes up or down depends on comparing the **[[Earnings yield]]** ($E/P$) to the **[[after-tax cost of debt]]**.

**The Rule:**

$$\text{If } \frac{E}{P} > r_d(1-t) \Rightarrow \text{EPS increases}$$

$$\text{If } \frac{E}{P} < r_d(1-t) \Rightarrow \text{EPS decreases}$$

$$\text{If } \frac{E}{P} = r_d(1-t) \Rightarrow \text{EPS unchanged}$$

**In plain English:** If the "return" on each share (earnings yield) exceeds the cost of the debt used to buy it back, EPS goes up. If the debt costs more than what each share "earns," EPS goes down.

**Worked Example:**

> **Selamat Plantations**
> - Shares: 2.2 million, EPS = MYR 3, Price = MYR 60
> - [[Earnings yield|Earnings yield]]: E/P = 3/60 = 5%
> - Borrow MYR 12 million to buy 200,000 shares at MYR 60

**Scenario A: After-tax [[Cost of debt|cost of debt]] = 5% (equals [[Earnings yield|earnings yield]])**

$$\text{New EPS} = \frac{MYR\,6.6M - (MYR\,12M \times 0.05)}{2.0M} = \frac{MYR\,6.0M}{2.0M} = MYR\,3.00$$

No change — the [[Cost of debt|cost of debt]] exactly offsets the benefit of fewer shares.

**Scenario B: After-tax [[Cost of debt|cost of debt]] = 6% (exceeds [[Earnings yield|earnings yield]])**

$$\text{New EPS} = \frac{MYR\,6.6M - (MYR\,12M \times 0.06)}{2.0M} = \frac{MYR\,5.88M}{2.0M} = MYR\,2.94$$

EPS **fell** because the debt costs more than what the retired shares were "earning."

> **Critical warning:** An increase in EPS from a buyback does **NOT** necessarily mean shareholder wealth increased. The same cash could have been paid as a dividend. An EPS boost from financial engineering is not the same as genuine value creation.

### Effect on [[Book value per share]] (BVPS)

**The Rule:**

- If **market price > BVPS** → repurchase **decreases** BVPS
- If **market price < BVPS** → repurchase **increases** BVPS

**Why?** When you buy back shares at a price *above* book value, you're paying more per share than the "accounting value" of each share, dragging down the average.

**Worked Example:**

> **Company A:** BVPS = $10, Market Price = $20 (price > book)
> - 10 million shares, $100M book equity
> - Buys back 250,000 shares at $20 ($5M)
> - New book equity: $100M − $5M = $95M
> - New shares: 9.75M
> - **New BVPS: $95M / 9.75M = $9.74** (decreased from $10)

> **Company B:** BVPS = $30, Market Price = $20 (price < book)
> - 10 million shares, $300M book equity
> - Buys back 250,000 shares at $20 ($5M)
> - New book equity: $300M − $5M = $295M
> - New shares: 9.75M
> - **New BVPS: $295M / 9.75M = $30.26** (increased from $30)

## 10. Valuation Equivalence: Cash Dividends vs. Share Repurchases

### The Core Principle

**Under identical assumptions (same tax treatment, same information content), paying $X in dividends creates the same shareholder wealth as repurchasing $X worth of shares.**

**Worked Example:**

> **Rohit Chemical Industries:** 10 million shares at Rs 200 each. Rs 500 million in free cash.

**Option 1: Cash Dividend of Rs 50/share**

$$\text{Ex-dividend price} = Rs\,200 - Rs\,50 = Rs\,150$$

$$\text{Shareholder wealth (1 share)} = Rs\,50 \text{ (cash)} + Rs\,150 \text{ (stock)} = Rs\,200$$

**Option 2: [[Share repurchase|Share Repurchase]] of Rs 500M**

$$\text{Shares bought back} = \frac{Rs\,500M}{Rs\,200} = 2.5M \text{ shares}$$

$$\text{Post-repurchase price} = \frac{(10M \times Rs\,200) - Rs\,500M}{10M - 2.5M} = \frac{Rs\,1{,}500M}{7.5M} = Rs\,200$$

$$\text{Shareholder wealth (1 share)} = Rs\,200$$

> **Same result either way.** The form of the payout doesn't matter — only the amount.

### Why Companies May Prefer Repurchases Over Dividends

| Factor | Favors Repurchases? | Explanation |
|---|---|---|
| **Tax advantages** | ✅ | Capital gains often taxed at lower rates or can be deferred |
| **Flexibility** | ✅ | No obligation to continue; no "expectation" is set |
| **Signaling** | ✅ | Signals management thinks shares are undervalued |
| **Offset stock option dilution** | ✅ | Repurchasing shares offsets new shares from employee options |
| **Increase leverage** | ✅ | Reduces equity, increasing [[Debt-to-equity ratio]] |
| **Dividend track record** | ❌ | Dividends show long-term commitment; many investors value this |
| **Investor income needs** | ❌ | Some investors (retirees, [[Endowments|endowments]]) prefer predictable cash income |

### Effect of Repurchases on [[Financial leverage]]

**Worked Example — Deira Oasis Holdings:**

> - Starting: AED 30M debt, AED 70M equity, AED 100M total cap (30% debt ratio)
> - Repurchases AED 7M of equity

| | Before | Buyback w/ Cash | Buyback w/ Debt |
|---|---|---|---|
| Debt | AED 30M (30%) | AED 30M (32%) | AED 37M (37%) |
| Equity | AED 70M (70%) | AED 63M (68%) | AED 63M (63%) |
| Total | AED 100M | AED 93M | AED 100M |

> Leverage increases either way, but **debt-financed buybacks increase leverage much more**.

## 11. Analysis of Dividend Safety

### How to Tell If a Company Can Sustain Its Dividend

#### Traditional Measures

**[[Dividend payout ratio]]:**

$$\text{Dividend Payout Ratio} = \frac{\text{Dividends}}{\text{Net Income}}$$

**[[Dividend coverage ratio]]:**

$$\text{Dividend Coverage Ratio} = \frac{\text{Net Income}}{\text{Dividends}}$$

**Typical benchmarks:**
- Mature companies target 40–60% payout (coverage of 1.7x–2.5x)
- Coverage dropping toward **1.0x** = dividend in jeopardy
- Coverage below 1.0x = company is paying dividends out of savings or borrowing

**Worked Example:**

> [[Net income|Net income]]: €100M, Dividends: €40M
>
> $$\text{Payout} = \frac{€40M}{€100M} = 40\%$$
>
> $$\text{Coverage} = \frac{€100M}{€40M} = 2.5\text{x}$$

#### Cash Flow-Based Measures (More Reliable)

[[Net income|Net income]] can be manipulated by accounting choices. **[[Free cash flow to equity]] ([[Free cash flow to equity|FCFE]])** is a better measure of what's actually available:

$$\text{FCFE} = \text{CFO} - \text{FCInv} + \text{Net Borrowing}$$

Where:
- **CFO** = [[CFA_Glossary/Cash flow from operations]] — cash the business actually generates
- **FCInv** = [[Fixed Capital Investment]] (capital expenditures) — cash needed to maintain/grow the business
- **Net Borrowing** = new debt issued minus debt repaid

$$\text{FCFE Coverage Ratio} = \frac{\text{FCFE}}{\text{Dividends} + \text{Share Repurchases}}$$

| FCFE Coverage | Interpretation |
|---|---|
| **> 1.0x** | Company is building cash reserves — sustainable |
| **= 1.0x** | Returning all available cash — tight but OK |
| **< 1.0x** | Paying out more than it generates — **NOT sustainable** |

**Worked Example — Lygon Resources:**

| Year | 2015 | 2016 | 2017 | 2018 |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Earnings Coverage (NI/Div) | 4.46x | 1.79x | 1.44x | 1.06x |
| FCFE (A$M) | 163 | 368 | 462 | 294 |
| FCFE Coverage | 1.35x | 1.02x | **0.83x** | **0.91x** |

> Both coverage ratios deteriorated steadily. By 2017, the FCFE coverage dropped below 1.0x — the company was **borrowing or drawing down cash** to fund distributions. The stock repurchase program was wisely discontinued, but even the dividend alone was barely covered by 2018.

### Warning Signs of an Unsustainable Dividend

- **Declining coverage ratios** (both earnings-based and FCFE-based)
- **Extremely high [[Dividend yield|dividend yield]]** relative to historical norm (market is pricing in an expected cut)
- **Rising debt to fund dividends** — borrowing to pay shareholders is never sustainable
- **Cutting productive capital spending** to maintain dividends — mortgaging the future
- **[[Payout ratio|Payout ratio]] near or above 100%** — paying more in dividends than the company earns

> **Real-World Example:** In 2019, Singapore's StarHub had a 9.4% [[Dividend yield|dividend yield]] — wildly above normal. Analysts projected shareholder equity would go to **zero** if the fixed dividend continued. StarHub cut to a variable 80% of net profit policy. The market had been screaming "this dividend is unsustainable" through the abnormally [[High yield|high yield]].

## Key Formulas Summary

| Formula | Expression |
|---|---|
| **[[Dividend payout ratio|Dividend Payout Ratio]]** | $\frac{\text{Dividends}}{\text{Net Income}}$ |
| **[[Dividend coverage ratio|Dividend Coverage Ratio]]** | $\frac{\text{Net Income}}{\text{Dividends}}$ |
| **Target Payout Adjustment (Lintner)** | $D_1 = D_0 + [(E_1 \times \text{Target Payout} - D_0) \times \text{Adj. Factor}]$ |
| **Constant Payout Dividend** | $D = \text{EPS} \times \text{Payout Ratio}$ |
| **Effective Double Tax Rate** | $\frac{\text{Corp Tax} + \text{Personal Tax on Div}}{\text{Pretax Earnings}}$ |
| **EPS After Debt-Funded Buyback** | $\frac{\text{Earnings} - (\text{Debt} \times r_d(1-t))}{\text{Shares After Repurchase}}$ |
| **FCFE** | $\text{CFO} - \text{FCInv} + \text{Net Borrowing}$ |
| **FCFE Coverage Ratio** | $\frac{\text{FCFE}}{\text{Dividends} + \text{Share Repurchases}}$ |
| **BVPS After Repurchase** | $\frac{\text{Book Equity} - \text{Repurchase Amount}}{\text{Shares} - \text{Shares Repurchased}}$ |
| **Post-Repurchase Share Price** | $\frac{\text{MV Equity} - \text{Cash Used}}{\text{Shares} - \text{Shares Repurchased}}$ |

## Quick Decision Framework

```
Is the company mature with strong, stable cash flows?
├── YES → Consider dividends + buybacks
│   ├── Volatile earnings? → Constant payout ratio or low stable dividend + special dividends
│   ├── Stable earnings? → Stable dividend policy (Lintner model)
│   └── Lots of excess cash, weak governance? → Pay it out (Jensen's hypothesis)
│
└── NO (growth company, needs cash for investment)
    └── Don't pay dividends. Retain and reinvest.
        └── If excess cash accumulates → Share repurchases (flexible, no commitment)
```
