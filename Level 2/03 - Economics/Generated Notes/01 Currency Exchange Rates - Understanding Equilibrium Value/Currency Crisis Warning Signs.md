# Currency Crisis Warning Signs

## The Real-World Analogy

Think of a currency like a bank with a line outside it.

Most of the time, people believe the bank can meet withdrawals, so nobody panics. But if depositors start to suspect the bank does not have enough cash, each depositor wants to withdraw before everyone else. The fear becomes self-reinforcing.

A [[Currency crisis]] works the same way. If investors believe a currency is overvalued, reserves are running down, inflation is rising, or banks cannot survive depreciation, they may sell the currency before others do. That selling pressure can create the very depreciation investors feared.

That is why CFA separates two ideas:

| Cause Type | Plain English | Exam Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| Fundamentals-based crisis | Weak economic data gradually makes the currency vulnerable | Warning signs may be observable in advance |
| Self-fulfilling crisis | Investors lose confidence and rush for the exit | Even decent fundamentals may not prevent a crisis |

The exam point is not that every warning sign guarantees a crisis. The point is that several warning signs together make the currency fragile.

## Definition

A [[Currency crisis]] is a sudden, sharp depreciation of a country's currency, often accompanied by [[Capital flight]], falling asset prices, pressure on [[Foreign exchange reserves]], and sometimes a [[Banking crisis]].

The crisis usually begins when investors conclude that the existing exchange rate cannot be maintained.

```text
Currency looks vulnerable
  |
  v
Investors sell domestic currency
  |
  v
Currency depreciates or peg comes under pressure
  |
  v
Authorities defend with reserves, higher rates, or controls
  |
  v
If defense fails, depreciation accelerates
```

## The CFA Warning Signs

| Warning Sign | What It Means | Why It Signals Crisis Risk |
|---|---|---|
| Deteriorating [[Terms of trade]] | Export prices weaken relative to import prices | The country earns less foreign currency from trade |
| Fixed or partially fixed exchange rate | The currency is pegged or heavily managed | The peg can become a target if markets doubt it is sustainable |
| Dramatic decline in [[Foreign exchange reserves]] | The central bank is spending reserves to support the currency | The country is running out of ammunition |
| Currency value above its historical mean | The currency appears overvalued | Exports become less competitive and depreciation pressure builds |
| Rising [[Inflation]] | Domestic prices rise faster than trading partners' prices | The currency loses real competitiveness |
| Liberalized capital markets | Capital can enter and leave freely | Inflows can reverse suddenly into outflows |
| Large foreign capital inflows | Foreign funding rises quickly, often into credit or asset markets | The eventual reversal can become sudden capital flight |
| Rising money supply relative to bank reserves | Domestic credit/money grows faster than reserve backing | The financial system becomes less able to withstand withdrawals or FX pressure |
| [[Banking crisis]] | Banks are under stress or insolvent | Currency depreciation can worsen foreign-currency debt burdens |

## Why Each Warning Sign Matters

### 1. Deteriorating Terms Of Trade

The [[Terms of trade]] compare export prices with import prices.

```text
Terms of trade deteriorate
  |
  v
Exports buy fewer imports
  |
  v
Country earns less foreign currency
  |
  v
Current account pressure worsens
  |
  v
Currency becomes more vulnerable
```

The mechanism is simple: if a country relies on exports to obtain foreign currency, weaker export prices make it harder to pay for imports, service foreign debt, and maintain confidence in the exchange rate.

Exam trap: an **improvement** in terms of trade is not a warning sign. CFA may offer it as a tempting opposite answer.

### 2. Fixed Or Partially Fixed Exchange Rate

A fixed exchange rate can create confidence when markets believe the peg is credible. But it also creates a visible target.

```text
Pegged exchange rate
  |
  v
Market sees an official defense level
  |
  v
If fundamentals weaken, investors test the peg
  |
  v
Central bank must spend reserves or raise rates
  |
  v
If defense is not credible, crisis risk rises
```

Under a [[Floating exchange rate]], adjustment can happen gradually. Under a peg, pressure may accumulate until the central bank is forced into a large devaluation.

Exam implication: fixed or partially fixed regimes are warning signs because they reduce exchange-rate flexibility and invite speculative pressure when the peg looks misaligned.

### 3. Dramatic Decline In Foreign Exchange Reserves

[[Foreign exchange reserves]] are the central bank's ammunition.

When a central bank defends a weakening currency, it may sell foreign reserves and buy domestic currency.

```text
Currency under depreciation pressure
  |
  v
Central bank sells foreign reserves
  |
  v
Central bank buys domestic currency
  |
  v
Reserve stock falls
  |
  v
Markets question how long the defense can continue
```

The problem is that reserves are finite. A sharp decline tells investors that the central bank may soon be unable to defend the currency. That can accelerate capital flight because investors rush to exit before reserves run out.

Exam trap: the level of reserves matters, but the **speed of decline** is especially important.

### 4. Currency Above Its Historical Mean

If a currency has appreciated substantially above its historical or mean-reverting level, it may be overvalued.

```text
Currency overvalued
  |
  v
Exports become expensive
  |
  v
Imports become cheap
  |
  v
Trade balance weakens
  |
  v
Pressure for future depreciation rises
```

Overvaluation is dangerous because it can make the country's external position look stable only while capital keeps flowing in. If capital inflows stop, the currency may need to fall sharply to restore competitiveness.

Exam implication: a currency that is substantially above its historical mean is a warning sign, not evidence of permanent strength.

### 5. Rising Inflation

High or rising [[Inflation]] erodes a currency's purchasing power.

```text
Domestic inflation rises
  |
  v
Domestic goods become more expensive versus foreign goods
  |
  v
Real exchange rate appreciates
  |
  v
Exports lose competitiveness
  |
  v
Markets expect nominal depreciation
```

Inflation also damages confidence. If investors believe policymakers will monetize deficits or fail to control money growth, they demand compensation or leave the currency.

Exam trap: in the [[Mundell-Fleming Model]], inflation is not the main short-run channel. But for currency crisis warning signs, rising inflation is directly relevant.

### 6. Liberalized Capital Markets

Capital market liberalization allows foreign money to enter and exit more freely.

That can be helpful during the inflow phase:

```text
Capital markets liberalized
  |
  v
Foreign capital enters
  |
  v
Asset prices rise and currency appreciates
```

But the same openness becomes dangerous when sentiment changes:

```text
Confidence weakens
  |
  v
Foreign capital exits quickly
  |
  v
Domestic assets are sold
  |
  v
Domestic currency is sold
  |
  v
Currency depreciates sharply
```

The mechanism is reversibility. The easier it was for capital to flow in, the easier it may be for capital to leave.

### 7. Large Foreign Capital Inflows

Large foreign capital inflows can make a country look strong before the crisis.

```text
Foreign capital inflows
  |
  v
Domestic asset prices rise
  |
  v
Credit expands and the currency appreciates
  |
  v
External debt and financial fragility build
  |
  v
If sentiment reverses, capital exits quickly
```

This is especially dangerous when inflows are short term or foreign-currency denominated. If the domestic currency begins to fall, borrowers need more domestic currency to service foreign-currency obligations. That can turn a currency decline into a balance-sheet crisis.

Exam trap: a pre-crisis economy may show an asset boom, credit boom, or strong capital inflows. Those are not automatically signs of health if they create fragile external financing.

### 8. Rising Money Supply Relative To Bank Reserves

An increase in money supply relative to bank reserves signals that domestic credit and liquidity are expanding faster than the banking system's reserve base.

```text
Money supply grows faster than bank reserves
  |
  v
Domestic credit expansion increases
  |
  v
Asset prices and imports may rise
  |
  v
Banks become more fragile
  |
  v
Currency confidence weakens
```

This matters because a fragile banking system and a fragile currency can reinforce each other. If depositors or foreign lenders lose confidence, banks may face funding pressure at the same time the currency comes under attack.

Exam implication: CFA may phrase this as an increase in the money supply relative to bank reserves. That is a warning sign.

### 9. Banking Crisis

A [[Banking crisis]] may precede, coincide with, or worsen a currency crisis.

The danger is especially severe when banks or borrowers have foreign-currency liabilities.

```text
Domestic currency depreciates
  |
  v
Foreign-currency debt becomes more expensive in domestic currency terms
  |
  v
Borrowers and banks suffer losses
  |
  v
Credit contracts and confidence falls
  |
  v
More capital leaves the country
```

This creates a feedback loop:

```text
Currency weakness -> bank stress -> capital flight -> more currency weakness
```

Exam trap: a banking crisis is not just a separate financial-sector event. It can be part of the same balance-sheet mechanism that turns depreciation into a broader macro crisis.

## How The Warning Signs Reinforce Each Other

A crisis usually does not come from one isolated indicator. It usually comes from a cluster.

```text
Capital market liberalization
  |
  v
Large capital inflows and currency appreciation
  |
  v
Currency rises above historical mean
  |
  v
Imports rise and terms of trade/current account weaken
  |
  v
Inflation or money growth rises
  |
  v
Banks build foreign-currency exposure
  |
  v
Confidence shifts
  |
  v
Capital outflows begin
  |
  v
Central bank spends FX reserves to defend currency
  |
  v
Reserves fall dramatically
  |
  v
Markets attack the currency or peg
  |
  v
Currency crisis
```

The deeper pattern:

```text
External vulnerability + weak confidence + limited reserves = crisis risk
```

## Fixed Exchange Rate Crisis Mechanics

For a fixed or partially fixed exchange rate, the crisis often looks like this:

```text
Official peg looks too strong
  |
  v
Speculators expect devaluation
  |
  v
They sell domestic currency
  |
  v
Central bank buys domestic currency using FX reserves
  |
  v
Reserves decline
  |
  v
If reserves become insufficient, peg breaks
  |
  v
Currency devalues sharply
```

The central bank can also raise interest rates to defend the currency, but that may damage the domestic economy and banking system. This is why defending a peg can become economically painful and politically unsustainable.

## Exam Traps

| Trap | Correction |
|---|---|
| Thinking one warning sign guarantees a crisis | CFA emphasizes combinations of warning signs and rising vulnerability. |
| Treating a fixed exchange rate as always safer | A peg can become a speculative target if it is not credible. |
| Ignoring reserve losses | Falling FX reserves show the central bank is using finite ammunition. |
| Calling improved terms of trade a warning sign | Deteriorating terms of trade are the warning sign. |
| Treating an overvalued currency as strength | A currency far above its historical mean may be vulnerable to reversal. |
| Forgetting banking-system links | Currency depreciation can worsen foreign-currency debt burdens and trigger banking stress. |
| Assuming crises are always predictable from fundamentals | Some crises can be self-fulfilling or driven by contagion. |

## Memory Hook

Use **RIFT-MBIC**:

```text
R - Reserves falling
I - Inflation rising
F - Fixed or semi-fixed exchange rate
T - Terms of trade deteriorating
M - Money supply rising relative to bank reserves
B - Banking crisis
I - Inflows after capital market liberalization
C - Currency above historical mean
```

If several of these appear together, the currency is fragile.

## Can I Solve This?

1. A country's terms of trade are deteriorating, reserves are falling rapidly, and the currency is pegged. Is this a crisis warning setup?

Answer: Yes. Export purchasing power is weakening, the central bank is spending reserves to defend the currency, and the peg gives markets a target to attack.

2. A country has liberalized capital markets and received large capital inflows. Why can this become dangerous?

Answer: The inflows may reverse quickly if confidence falls. Investors sell domestic assets and domestic currency, creating capital flight and depreciation pressure.

3. Why is a banking crisis linked to a currency crisis?

Answer: If banks or borrowers owe foreign-currency debt, depreciation increases the domestic-currency value of liabilities. That worsens balance sheets, undermines confidence, and can trigger more capital outflows.

4. Which is a warning sign: improving terms of trade or deteriorating terms of trade?

Answer: Deteriorating terms of trade.
