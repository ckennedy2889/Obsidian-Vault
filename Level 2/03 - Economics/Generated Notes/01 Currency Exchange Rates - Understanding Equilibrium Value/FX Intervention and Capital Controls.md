# FX Intervention and Capital Controls

## The Real-World Analogy

Imagine a small town with one main road in and out.

When too many cars enter too quickly, traffic jams, housing prices spike, and the town overheats. When all the cars suddenly leave, local businesses collapse.

Capital flows can behave the same way. Foreign money can flood into a country, push up asset prices, strengthen the currency, and fuel credit growth. Then, if confidence changes, that same money can leave quickly and create currency stress.

[[Capital Controls]] and FX intervention are policy tools for managing that flow.

## Definition

FX intervention occurs when a central bank or government acts in the foreign exchange market to influence the value or volatility of its currency.

Capital controls are restrictions, taxes, or administrative rules designed to influence cross-border capital flows.

## Why Policymakers Intervene

CFA emphasizes three main objectives:

| Objective | Why It Matters |
|---|---|
| Prevent excessive currency appreciation | Over-appreciation can hurt exporters and create asset bubbles |
| Preserve monetary policy independence | Policymakers may want to set rates without triggering destabilizing capital flows |
| Reduce foreign capital inflows | Excess inflows can fuel credit booms, asset bubbles, and later capital flight |
| Reduce asset price volatility | Sudden capital outflows can sharply reduce stock and bond prices |
| Maintain a fixed exchange rate | Capital restrictions make it easier to defend a target exchange rate |
| Protect strategic industries | Governments may restrict foreign ownership in sensitive sectors |

## Push And Pull Factors

Capital flows are driven by both external and domestic forces.

| Pull Factors | Mechanism |
|---|---|
| Relative price stability | Investors prefer currencies with credible inflation control |
| Flexible exchange-rate regime | Reduces fear of sudden one-time devaluation |
| Improved fiscal position | Lowers sovereign risk |
| Privatization | Creates investable assets |
| Better domestic growth prospects | Raises expected returns |

| Push Factors | Mechanism |
|---|---|
| Low global interest rates | Investors search for higher yields abroad |
| Portfolio diversification | Investors allocate capital internationally |
| Global risk-on sentiment | Capital moves toward riskier markets |

The risk is that push factors can reverse even if domestic fundamentals have not changed much.

## Why Excess Capital Inflows Can Be Dangerous

```text
Large capital inflows
  |
  v
Currency appreciates
  |
  v
Exports lose competitiveness
  |
  v
Asset prices and credit expand
  |
  v
External debt and fragility rise
  |
  v
If flows reverse, currency crisis risk rises
```

The country can look strong before it becomes fragile.

## Direct FX Market Intervention

Direct intervention means the central bank buys or sells currencies.

### To Resist Appreciation

If the domestic currency is appreciating too much:

```text
Central bank sells domestic currency
Central bank buys foreign currency
Domestic currency supply rises
Domestic currency appreciation pressure falls
FX reserves increase
```

This type of intervention is easier in a narrow technical sense because the central bank can create domestic currency.

### To Resist Depreciation

If the domestic currency is depreciating too much:

```text
Central bank sells foreign reserves
Central bank buys domestic currency
Domestic currency demand rises
Depreciation pressure falls
FX reserves decline
```

This type of intervention is limited by the central bank's stock of [[Foreign exchange reserves]].

## Capital Controls

Capital controls can target either inflows or outflows.

Examples include:

| Control Type | Purpose |
|---|---|
| Taxes on foreign exchange transactions | Discourage short-term speculative flows |
| Required non-interest-bearing deposits | Reduce the effective return to foreign investors |
| Limits on foreign ownership | Slow foreign capital inflows into domestic assets |
| Restrictions on capital outflows | Prevent sudden capital flight |
| Multiple exchange rates | Subsidize or penalize specific transaction types |

Controls can buy time, but they may also distort markets and reduce investor confidence.

## Sterilized vs Unsterilized Intervention

When a central bank intervenes to resist appreciation, it often buys foreign currency and sells domestic currency.

### Unsterilized Intervention

```text
Central bank buys foreign currency
  |
  v
Central bank sells newly created domestic currency
  |
  v
Domestic money supply rises
  |
  v
Domestic interest rates may fall
  |
  v
Inflation risk may rise
```

Unsterilized intervention changes the domestic money supply.

### Sterilized Intervention

In sterilized intervention, the central bank offsets the money-supply effect.

```text
Central bank buys foreign currency
  |
  v
Domestic money supply initially rises
  |
  v
Central bank sells domestic securities
  |
  v
Domestic liquidity is drained back out
```

Sterilized intervention tries to affect the exchange rate without changing the money supply.

The limitation is that sterilization requires enough domestic securities and market capacity. It may be difficult to sustain if capital inflows are persistent and large.

## Effectiveness

| Market Type | Intervention Effectiveness | Reason |
|---|---|---|
| Developed markets | Usually limited for changing exchange-rate levels | FX trading volume is large relative to official reserves |
| Emerging markets | Potentially more effective, but still imperfect | Reserves may be large relative to local FX turnover |

The exam point:

```text
Effectiveness depends on central bank resources relative to market trading volume.
```

Intervention may be better at reducing short-term volatility than permanently changing a currency's fundamental value.

## The Impossible Trinity Link

A country cannot simultaneously maintain:

```text
1. Free capital mobility
2. Fixed exchange rate
3. Independent monetary policy
```

It can choose only two.

Capital controls are one way to preserve more monetary policy independence while managing the exchange rate.

```text
Want fixed exchange rate + independent monetary policy?
  |
  v
Must restrict capital mobility
```

## Exam Traps

| Trap | Correction |
|---|---|
| Assuming intervention is equally effective everywhere | It is usually less effective in developed markets because FX turnover is huge. |
| Forgetting reserve limits | Defending against depreciation is limited by FX reserves. |
| Treating capital inflows as always good | Excess inflows can cause overvaluation, credit booms, and later capital flight. |
| Ignoring push factors | Capital can leave because of global conditions, not only domestic weakness. |
| Assuming controls are costless | Controls can distort markets, reduce confidence, and lower liquidity. |
| Confusing volatility reduction with level control | Intervention may smooth volatility without changing long-run fair value. |
| Forgetting sterilization | Sterilized intervention offsets the money-supply impact; unsterilized intervention does not. |
| Assuming controls block all flows | Trade-finance-related flows often continue even under controls. |
| Ignoring long-term reputation costs | Controls can make future foreign capital more expensive or less available. |

