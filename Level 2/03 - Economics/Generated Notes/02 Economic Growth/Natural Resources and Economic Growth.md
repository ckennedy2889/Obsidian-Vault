# Natural Resources and Economic Growth

## The Real-World Analogy

Natural resources are like being born with a valuable inheritance.

An inheritance can help if it is invested wisely. But it can also make people lazy, attract conflict, and distract from building skills. Countries work the same way.

The CFA point:

```text
Natural resources can help growth.
Natural resources are not required for growth.
Natural resources can even inhibit growth.
```

## Core Rule

Limited ownership of natural resources does not necessarily constrain economic growth because countries can access resources through [[International Trade]].

Resource-poor countries can grow rapidly if they have:

| Growth Driver | Why It Matters |
|---|---|
| Strong institutions | Protects property rights and encourages investment |
| Human capital | Raises productivity and innovation |
| Technology | Improves output per worker |
| Trade access | Allows resource imports without domestic ownership |
| Competitive industries | Supports exports and scale economies |

Technological progress also weakens the resource-constraint argument. Better technology can improve resource efficiency, enable substitution away from scarce inputs, and reduce the share of raw resources needed per unit of GDP.

## Why Resource Ownership Is Not Necessary

Ownership and access are different.

```text
Resource ownership = the resource is inside your borders.
Resource access = you can buy or import the resource.
```

A country can lack oil, minerals, or fertile land but still import inputs and specialize in high-value production. The limiting factor becomes the ability to transform inputs into valuable output.

## The Resource Curse

Some resource-rich countries grow slowly because resource wealth can distort incentives.

| Resource Curse Channel | Mechanism |
|---|---|
| Rent-seeking | People fight over resource income instead of producing new value |
| Corruption | Concentrated resource revenues weaken institutions |
| Political instability | Control of resources can fund conflict |
| Weak diversification | The economy depends too heavily on one commodity |
| Dutch disease | Resource exports appreciate the currency and hurt other tradable sectors |

## Dutch Disease

[[Dutch disease]] occurs when strong resource exports cause currency appreciation that makes other industries less competitive.

```text
Resource boom
  |
  v
Foreign demand for domestic currency rises
  |
  v
Currency appreciates
  |
  v
Manufacturing and non-resource exports become expensive
  |
  v
Non-resource tradable sectors shrink
  |
  v
Economy becomes less diversified
```

The problem appears when commodity prices fall:

```text
Commodity price falls
  |
  v
Resource revenue falls
  |
  v
Non-resource export base is weak
  |
  v
Growth slows sharply
```

## Exam Traps

| Trap | Correction |
|---|---|
| Saying natural resources are necessary for growth | Access through trade can substitute for ownership. |
| Saying natural resources always help | They can create Dutch disease, corruption, and weak diversification. |
| Treating resource scarcity as a hard constraint | Institutions, human capital, trade, and technology often matter more. |
| Ignoring substitution | Technology can reduce dependence on scarce resources. |
| Forgetting counterexamples | Resource-poor economies can become wealthy; resource-rich economies can stagnate. |
| Calling all resource wealth bad | Good institutions can turn resource wealth into long-run capital. |

## Memory Hook

```text
Resources are useful inputs, not destiny.
Institutions decide whether resources become fuel or a trap.
```
