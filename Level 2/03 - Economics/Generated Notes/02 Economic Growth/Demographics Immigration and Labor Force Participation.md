# Demographics Immigration and Labor Force Participation

## The Real-World Analogy

An economy's labor force is like the number of people available to row a boat.

More rowers can make the boat move faster in total. But the speed per rower depends on skill, tools, coordination, and technology.

That is why CFA separates:

```text
Total GDP growth
from
GDP per capita or output per worker growth
```

## Labor Input

The quantity of labor depends on:

```text
Labor input = labor force x average hours worked
```

The labor force is the working-age population that is employed or actively seeking work.

Growth in labor input raises total output, but it does not automatically raise output per capita.

## Main Labor Supply Drivers

| Driver | Effect On Growth |
|---|---|
| Population growth | Increases potential labor supply |
| Age structure | Younger working-age populations support future labor force growth |
| Fertility rate | Affects future population and labor force size |
| Labor force participation | Determines how much working-age population enters the labor force |
| Immigration | Can offset aging and low fertility |
| Average hours worked | More hours raise labor input; fewer hours reduce it |

## Demographics

Demographics affect potential GDP because they determine future labor supply.

```text
Younger population
  |
  v
More future workers
  |
  v
Higher potential labor input
  |
  v
Higher potential GDP growth
```

An aging population creates the opposite pressure:

```text
Aging population
  |
  v
More retirees relative to workers
  |
  v
Labor force growth slows or turns negative
  |
  v
Potential GDP growth slows
```

## Labor Force Participation

The labor force participation rate is:

$$
\text{LFPR} = \frac{\text{Labor force}}{\text{Working-age population}}
$$

Higher participation increases labor input without requiring a larger population.

Unlike pure population growth, a higher labor force participation rate can raise output per capita because more of the population is producing output.

Examples:

| Participation Change | Growth Effect |
|---|---|
| More women entering the workforce | Raises labor input |
| Older workers delaying retirement | Raises labor input |
| Discouraged workers leaving labor force | Lowers labor input |
| High labor taxes reducing work incentives | Can lower labor input |

## Immigration

Immigration can help countries facing aging populations or low fertility.

```text
Immigration
  |
  v
Working-age population rises
  |
  v
Labor force increases
  |
  v
Potential GDP rises
```

High-skilled immigration can also raise [[Human capital]] and [[Total Factor Productivity (TFP)]] through innovation and knowledge spillovers.

The short-run caveat is the [[Output Gap]]. If an economy already has a large negative output gap and high unemployment, additional labor supply may increase unemployment pressure before demand and capital adjust.

## Average Hours Worked

Average hours worked often decline as countries become wealthier.

Possible reasons:

| Reason | Mechanism |
|---|---|
| Wealth effect | Higher income lets workers choose more leisure |
| Labor laws | Limits on work hours reduce total hours |
| Higher labor taxes | Reduces after-tax reward for working |
| More part-time work | Lowers average hours per worker |

Lower average hours can reduce total labor input even when employment is high.

## Total GDP vs Per Capita GDP

This is the main exam trap.

More workers can raise total GDP:

```text
More labor -> more output
```

But per capita GDP depends on output per person.

If population rises as fast as output, living standards may not improve.

```text
Labor force grows
  |
  v
Total GDP rises
  |
  v
Per capita GDP rises only if productivity also improves
```

## Exam Traps

| Trap | Correction |
|---|---|
| Assuming population growth raises living standards | It raises total output, not necessarily per capita output. |
| Ignoring age structure | Working-age population matters more than total population. |
| Treating immigration as only population growth | Skilled immigration can also raise human capital and TFP. |
| Ignoring the output gap | Immigration is more growth-supportive when the economy can absorb additional labor. |
| Forgetting average hours worked | Labor input can fall even if the number of workers rises. |
| Confusing labor force with population | Labor force includes working-age people employed or seeking work. |

## Memory Hook

```text
Labor growth = people x participation x hours.
Living-standard growth needs productivity too.
```
